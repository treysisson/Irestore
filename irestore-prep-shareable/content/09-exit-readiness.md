## Why This Matters Now
<!-- id: exit-why-now -->

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Building a Sellable Business = Building a Better Business</div>
  <p>Whether we exit in 2 years or 5, the actions that make iRESTORE "exit-ready" are the same actions that make it a more valuable, more resilient, better-run company. Every improvement here increases enterprise value regardless of timing.</p>
  <p>This framework draws on M&amp;A advisory best practices from firms that have completed $50B+ in tech acquisitions across 1,500+ deals. The principles are tailored to iRESTORE's specific situation: a profitable, high-growth DTC company with expanding channels and product lines.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">The Core Principle</div>
  <p><strong>The ops side of the business isn't just value protection — it's enterprise value creation.</strong></p>
  <p>Saving 5 points of margin on a $200M business, at a 10x multiple, creates $100M in enterprise value. Every operational improvement compounds through the exit multiple.</p>
</div>

## Founder Dependency Audit
<!-- id: exit-founder-dependency -->

<p>The #1 factor that reduces valuation multiples: buyer perception that the business can't run without the founder. Buyers assess this by looking at:</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Key Questions Buyers Will Ask</div>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Decision authority:</strong> What gets signed off by Kevin personally? Can leadership make decisions autonomously?</li>
    <li><strong>Key account relationships:</strong> Is Kevin the one closing major deals, managing key retail relationships (Costco, QVC)?</li>
    <li><strong>Retention &amp; CX:</strong> Is Kevin involved in customer escalations, or does the team handle this independently?</li>
    <li><strong>Marketing &amp; brand:</strong> Is Kevin the face of the brand? How much of our content, social, and PR depends on him personally?</li>
    <li><strong>Product innovation:</strong> Does the product pipeline depend on Kevin's vision, or is there a product team that drives this?</li>
    <li><strong>Strategic direction:</strong> Would the company's strategy be unclear if Kevin stepped back for a month?</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">The 30-Day Test</div>
  <p><strong>Could Kevin truly walk away from the business for 30 days?</strong> If things would break, stop growing, or lose direction — those are the areas to fix first. The goal isn't to remove Kevin; it's to ensure the business has an engine that keeps running and growing even when he's focused elsewhere.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Action Items</div>
  <ol>
    <li>Map every decision that currently requires Kevin's sign-off. Identify which can be delegated with clear guardrails.</li>
    <li>Document Kevin's key relationships (retail buyers, suppliers, partners). Create transition plans for each.</li>
    <li>Ensure marketing channels aren't dependent on Kevin as the face — or build a transition plan if they are.</li>
    <li>Establish a product roadmap process that captures Kevin's vision but doesn't require his day-to-day involvement in every SKU decision.</li>
  </ol>
</div>

## Financial Clean-Up &amp; Presentation
<!-- id: exit-financials -->

<p>The most common issue across companies of all sizes: accounting practices that don't hold up to buyer scrutiny. This doesn't mean the numbers are wrong — it means they're not presented in a way that maximizes perceived value.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Common Issues to Audit</div>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Cash vs. accrual consistency:</strong> Mixing methods is extremely common and immediately flagged by acquirers. Ensure consistent accrual accounting across all entities.</li>
    <li><strong>Add-backs &amp; adjustments:</strong> Identify legitimate EBITDA add-backs (one-time expenses, founder comp above market, non-recurring costs). These directly increase the valuation.</li>
    <li><strong>Channel economics:</strong> Clean P&amp;L by channel (DTC, Amazon, Costco, QVC, B2B). Buyers want to see contribution margin per channel, not just blended numbers.</li>
    <li><strong>Return rate accounting:</strong> With return rates of 10-30% depending on channel, how returns are accounted for significantly impacts revenue recognition and margin presentation.</li>
    <li><strong>Related-party transactions:</strong> The Shenzhen factory relationship (father's facility, net-150 terms) needs to be clearly documented and at arm's-length pricing. Buyers will scrutinize this.</li>
    <li><strong>Transfer pricing:</strong> Costs allocated from overseas affiliates should be based on documented transfer pricing policies.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Quick Win: AI-Powered Financial Audit</div>
  <p>Upload our P&amp;L and balance sheet to an AI tool with the prompt: <em>"I am a founder considering selling my business. Please look at my P&amp;L and tell me what a buyer might identify or question."</em> This won't replace proper accounting, but it will flag the obvious issues quickly.</p>
</div>

## Process Documentation
<!-- id: exit-processes -->

<p>Buyers pay premium multiples for businesses with documented, repeatable processes. This doesn't require a 500-page manual — it requires clarity on how the business actually operates.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Priority Areas to Document</div>
  <table class="data-table" style="width:100%; margin-top:8px;">
    <thead>
      <tr><th>Area</th><th>Why It Matters</th><th>Format</th></tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr><td>Supply chain &amp; fulfillment</td><td>Shenzhen factory relationship, 3PL operations, inventory management</td><td>SOPs + flow diagrams</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Product launch playbook</td><td>How we go from concept to shelf — repeatable process signals scalability</td><td>Checklist + timeline template</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Channel onboarding</td><td>How we launch on a new channel (QVC, Costco inline, B2B clinics)</td><td>Playbook per channel</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Customer experience</td><td>Return handling, phone scripts, escalation paths, AI implementation</td><td>SOPs + call scripts</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Financial reporting</td><td>Monthly close process, KPI dashboards, board-ready reporting</td><td>Templates + cadence doc</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Hiring &amp; onboarding</td><td>With 100+ people across multiple countries, process matters</td><td>Hiring playbook</td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Documentation Tips</div>
  <p>There's no right format. Some people type, some make Loom videos, some use Notion. The best format is the one that actually gets done. Start with the processes that are most founder-dependent and work outward.</p>
  <p><strong>Bonus:</strong> The act of documenting processes almost always reveals inefficiencies. You'll find yourself saying "why are we doing it this way?" — and that's the point. It's a profitable exercise even if an exit is years away.</p>
</div>

## Revenue Quality &amp; Retention
<!-- id: exit-revenue-quality -->

<p>The single most cited factor in driving premium multiples: <strong>how sticky is the revenue?</strong> This is typically discussed in terms of net revenue retention (NRR) for subscription businesses, but the same principle applies to DTC hardware.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">iRESTORE's Revenue Quality Challenge</div>
  <p>Hair growth devices are a one-time purchase — there's no built-in recurring revenue. This naturally caps our multiple relative to subscription businesses. But there are ways to improve the revenue quality story:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Product ecosystem expansion:</strong> Body panels, red light belt, accessories, replacement parts. A customer who buys a helmet and later buys a body panel increases LTV.</li>
    <li><strong>Consumables / refills:</strong> Any recurring consumable product would dramatically change the revenue narrative for buyers.</li>
    <li><strong>B2B clinic subscriptions:</strong> If the clinic channel can be structured with recurring orders or service contracts, this becomes a retention-driven revenue stream.</li>
    <li><strong>Cohort analysis:</strong> Even without subscriptions, we can show buyers that customers who enter our ecosystem in Year 1 spend X% more in Year 2-3 through additional products.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Marketing Channel Diversification</div>
  <p>Buyers assess risk by looking at how concentrated our customer acquisition is. Key questions:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>What % of revenue comes from each channel (DTC, Amazon, Costco, QVC, B2B)?</li>
    <li>Within DTC, what % of acquisition comes from each marketing channel (Meta, Google, organic, influencer)?</li>
    <li>If we turned off paid ads tomorrow, what happens to revenue? How much is organic/repeat?</li>
    <li>Is Amazon serving as a "research platform" that drives DTC conversion? If so, can we quantify that?</li>
  </ul>
  <p>The more diversified the acquisition mix, the lower the perceived risk, the higher the multiple.</p>
</div>

## Building the Management Team
<!-- id: exit-management-team -->

<p>A company with a deep management bench commands a higher multiple than one where the top layer is thin. Buyers are purchasing a machine, not a collection of individuals — and the machine needs to run without any single person.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Current State: 3 Simultaneous Departures</div>
  <p>Daniel Ju (Chief of Staff), CX Director, and a Product Manager are all departing. This creates urgency but also opportunity — we get to rebuild these roles with exit-readiness in mind from day one.</p>
  <p><strong>Key principle:</strong> Every leadership hire from this point forward should be someone a buyer would want to keep. That means:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>People who can operate autonomously, not messengers</li>
    <li>People with domain expertise a buyer would value</li>
    <li>People who are retained by reasonable comp + equity packages (equity as a retention tool — this is why the phantom equity pool matters)</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Org Design for Exit</div>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Clear functional ownership:</strong> Each major area (ops, finance, CX, supply chain, product, marketing) has an accountable leader</li>
    <li><strong>No single points of failure:</strong> If any one person left, the function would continue operating</li>
    <li><strong>Documented roles &amp; responsibilities:</strong> Not just for compliance — so a buyer can look at the org chart and immediately understand how the company operates</li>
  </ul>
</div>

## Negotiation Principles for Exit
<!-- id: exit-negotiation -->

<p>Based on lessons from 1,500+ completed acquisitions at the $1M-$500M+ level.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">The #1 Deal Killer</div>
  <p><strong>Over-negotiation.</strong> Founders who show up with 25 points to negotiate when they really care about 3 things. The additional complexity stalls deals, burns goodwill, and often causes the other side to walk away.</p>
  <p><strong>The rule:</strong> If you have 10 things on your list, focus on 3. If you don't care about something, agree and move on. Once you've agreed, don't retrade.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Attorneys: Handle with Care</div>
  <p>Attorneys are incentivized to rack up hours, not close deals. They will flag every clause with a 1-in-10,000 chance of mattering. You need legal protection, but you don't need to die on every hill your attorney identifies.</p>
  <p><strong>Best practice:</strong> Decide what matters to you <em>before</em> engaging attorneys. Use them for targeted protection, not open-ended negotiation.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Fit &gt; Highest Bidder</div>
  <p>Sellers regularly take lower offers from buyers they trust. This matters especially when deal structure includes earnouts or performance-based payments — you need to trust that the buyer will run the business well enough for you to realize the full payout.</p>
  <p><strong>The cash test:</strong> Am I getting enough guaranteed cash that, regardless of what happens with the performance component, I won't regret this?</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">When You Get a Win, Take It</div>
  <p>If you want $10M and someone offers $12M, take the $12M and move on. Pushing for $15M because "they clearly want my business" is how deals fall apart. Buyers are financially disciplined and will retrade later if they feel squeezed.</p>
</div>
