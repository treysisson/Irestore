## Overview
<!-- id: jd-exec-overview -->

<p class="muted">The job description lays out four specific expectations for the first 90 days. Here's how I'd approach each one — not as abstract goals but as concrete work products with timelines.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">The Four 90-Day Expectations</div>
  <ol>

## 1. Operational Audit & Top 5 Improvement Areas
<!-- id: jd-exec-audit -->

<p>The goal isn't a 50-page consulting document. It's identifying the 5 things that, if fixed, create the most leverage at iRESTORE's current scale and growth rate.</p>

<h3>How I'd Run the Audit (Weeks 1-4)</h3>

<table>
  <tr><th>Week</th><th>Focus</th><th>Output</th></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Week 1</strong></td><td><strong>Financial deep dive</strong> — P&L by channel, gross margins by product line, COGS breakdown, return rate economics, ad spend efficiency. Work with the Controller (Angel Chao) to understand where money flows.</td><td>Financial reality map: where the margin is, where it leaks, what the real unit economics look like by channel × product</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Week 2</strong></td><td><strong>Process walkthroughs</strong> — shadow the CX team for a full day. Walk the warehouse end-to-end. Sit with whoever runs Amazon ops. Understand the Fulfil.io ERP setup and what's manual vs. automated.</td><td>Process gap list: where things break, where they're manual and shouldn't be, where handoffs create delays</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Week 3</strong></td><td><strong>People & capacity</strong> — 1:1s with every team lead (already in progress from commitment #2). Understand headcount vs. workload. Where is the team stretched? Where is there slack? Where are we one-deep on critical knowledge?</td><td>Capacity assessment: teams that are over/under-resourced relative to growth trajectory</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Week 4</strong></td><td><strong>Synthesis & prioritization</strong> — take everything from weeks 1-3 and score each issue on impact × urgency × effort. Stack rank.</td><td>Top 5 improvement areas with estimated impact, presented to Kevin</td></tr>
</table>

## 2. Build Relationships & Understand Culture
<!-- id: jd-exec-relationships -->

<p>This one doesn't have a deliverable — but it has a method. The relationships built in weeks 1-4 determine whether anything else on this list actually works.</p>

<h3>Approach</h3>

<table>
  <tr><th>Who</th><th>What I Want to Understand</th><th>How</th></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Brandon</strong> (Dir. of Ops)</td><td>What he's good at, what he's stretched on, how he wants to be managed. Kevin described him as reliable but not strategic — I need to understand that dynamic firsthand.</td><td>Work alongside him, not above him, in week 1. Joint warehouse walk. Ask him what he'd fix if he could.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Angel Chao</strong> (Controller)</td><td>Financial reporting cadence, what's clean and what's messy, where she needs support. She's new-ish and reports to a Fractional CFO — that's a lot of distance from decision-making.</td><td>Weekly standing meeting. Ask her to walk me through the close process. Let her tell me what's broken.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Daniel Ju</strong> (departing CoS)</td><td>Everything that lives in his head. 8.5 years of institutional knowledge. How Kevin actually works — not the JD version, the real version.</td><td>Structured knowledge transfer sessions. Record them (with permission). Focus on: what decisions need Kevin, what doesn't, what are the landmines.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Longchuan Huang</strong> (Head of Product Dev)</td><td>Product roadmap reality vs. aspiration. Manufacturing constraints. Where product and ops intersect. He's 3 months in — we're both new, which is an advantage.</td><td>Peer-level relationship. Compare notes on what we're both learning.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Junior Reyes</strong> (VP Growth & Data)</td><td>Data infrastructure, analytics capabilities, what data exists vs. what's actually used. He's remote (NYC) and tech-forward — a natural ally for AI/data initiatives.</td><td>Video calls, async collaboration. Share analytical frameworks. Find quick wins together.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>CX team leads</strong></td><td>Ground-level reality. What customers actually say. Where the scripts work and where they don't. What the outgoing director was doing right (not just what she wasn't).</td><td>Listen to calls. Read transcripts. Sit in on team meetings before changing anything.</td></tr>
</table>

<h3>Culture Assessment</h3>

<p>Three things I want to understand about the culture that you can't learn from a JD:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Speed vs. quality</strong> — does the team move fast and fix later, or plan carefully and move once? (Kevin's "fail fast" value suggests the former, but is that true at every level?)</li>
  <li><strong>Decision rights</strong> — what decisions can people make independently? The "Daniel as messenger" pattern suggests decisions may have been over-centralized.</li>
  <li><strong>How conflict surfaces</strong> — do problems come up in meetings, in Slack, in 1:1s with Kevin, or not at all until they explode?</li>
</ul>

## 3. Own Operational Meetings & Day-to-Day Decisions
<!-- id: jd-exec-meetings -->

<p>This is the "founder time liberation" commitment. Kevin should feel the weight come off his shoulders within 30 days — not 90.</p>

<h3>Phase-In Plan</h3>

<table>
  <tr><th>Timing</th><th>Action</th><th>Kevin's Role</th></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Week 1-2</strong></td><td>Observe all existing operational meetings. Understand the current rhythm, who attends, what gets discussed, what gets decided vs. deferred.</td><td>Business as usual — I'm watching and learning</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Week 3-4</strong></td><td>Begin co-leading operational meetings. Take ownership of agenda, follow-ups, and action items. Kevin still in the room but I'm driving.</td><td>Validates decisions, redirects if I'm off-base</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Week 5+</strong></td><td>Own meetings outright. Kevin gets a weekly summary (written, not verbal) and joins only when strategic decisions require his input.</td><td>Reads the summary. Steps in when needed. Otherwise, freed up.</td></tr>
</table>

<h3>What "Day-to-Day Decisions" Means in Practice</h3>

<p>Establishing a clear decision framework early so Kevin doesn't have to be asked about everything:</p>

<table>
  <tr><th>Decision Type</th><th>Who Decides</th><th>Example</th></tr>
  <tr style="background: var(--purple-light)"><td><strong>COO decides, informs Kevin</strong></td><td>Me</td><td>CX staffing changes, vendor negotiations under $X, process improvements, return handling escalations, operational tooling</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>COO recommends, Kevin approves</strong></td><td>Kevin</td><td>New hires above a certain level, channel-specific pricing, major vendor changes, budget above threshold</td></tr>
  <tr style="background: var(--cream)"><td><strong>Kevin decides, COO executes</strong></td><td>Kevin</td><td>Product direction, brand positioning, strategic partnerships, major capital allocation</td></tr>
</table>

<div class="tip"><strong>The anti-Daniel pattern:</strong> The goal isn't to become the new gatekeeper. It's to handle 80% of operational decisions autonomously, escalate the 15% that need Kevin's input with a recommendation (not just a question), and stay out of the 5% that are Kevin's domain (product, brand, big relationships).</div>
