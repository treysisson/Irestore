**Context:** 2+ weeks since Apr 16 counter call. You anchored at **$4M net at $300M exit** (<3% of total value). Kevin was positive on framing, took it to advisors. He's been traveling (China mfg meetings -> Europe) and texted proactively twice with updates. He's had the Excel for 2 weeks.

## Before anything: Read the room first

Don't open with comp. Let Kevin set the tone. He called this meeting — let him lead. Your opening should be warm and light:

> *"Hey Kevin, good to connect — how was the trip? China and Europe back to back sounds intense."*

Let him talk. You're listening for energy, not words. Is he relaxed and forward-leaning, or careful and hedging? That tells you which scenario you're in before he says a single number.

## Scenario A: Kevin meets or nearly meets the ask

**What it sounds like:**
- "I ran the numbers with my advisors and I think we can make $4M work"
- "I'm comfortable in the 3.5-4M range net"
- He leads with equity structure details, not a negotiation

**What it means:** He wants you. His advisors confirmed the ask is reasonable. The 2-week delay was him getting buy-in, not building a counter.

**Your move:**
- Express genuine enthusiasm — Kevin cares about people being excited, don't go full poker face here
- Immediately pivot to **structure and protections** — this is where the real value is now
- Key structural asks (pick your top 3):
  1. **Change of control: full single-trigger acceleration** (already agreed in principle)
  2. **Broad definition of "sale"** — covers asset sale, merger, recap, partial sale, IPO
  3. **Termination protection** — vested phantom survives termination, pays at next liquidity
- Say something like:

> *"Kevin, that's really meaningful and I appreciate it. I'm fired up. I want to make sure we button up the structure so there are no surprises for either of us down the road — can I walk you through the three things I care about most?"*

**Do NOT re-negotiate the percentage upward.** You got what you asked for. Close it.

## Scenario B: Kevin counters at 2.5-3.5% phantom (~$4.7-6.6M net at $400M)

**What it sounds like:**
- "My advisors think 3% is the right number for this role"
- "I can do 2.5% with a path to more based on performance"
- He frames it as generous relative to his other candidates

**What it means:** He's negotiating in good faith. His advisors probably anchored him to "2-3% for a first COO" and he's giving you the top of what they recommended. This is the most likely scenario.

**Your move — depends on the number:**

**If 3-3.5%:** You're in striking distance. Don't fight the base number — propose a tiered structure that gets you to 4.5-5%:

> *"I appreciate that, and I think 3% as a starting point shows we're aligned on the intent. Here's what I'd propose — what if we structure it as 3% base grant plus performance tiers that can take it to 5%? That way you're not giving away equity on day one to an unproven COO, and I have a path to the number that makes me feel like an owner. I'd tie the tiers to things we'd both want anyway — margin improvement, exit-readiness milestones."*

**If 2.5%:** Need to close a bigger gap. Lead with the after-tax math:

> *"I appreciate the transparency. Can I share one thing that might reframe this? At 2.5% phantom, after CA taxes, the net at a $300M exit is about $3.5M. That's below the tipping point we talked about. I'm not trying to nickel-and-dime — I just want us looking at the same math. Can we talk about how to bridge that gap?"*

Then propose the tiered structure (2.5% base + performance tiers to 4.5-5%).

## Scenario C: Kevin changes the equity vehicle (real equity, LLC units, etc.)

**What it sounds like:**
- "My advisors suggested we look at a different structure"
- "What if we did actual equity instead of phantom?"
- "We're restructuring and there might be a way to do real shares"

**What it means:** This is potentially very good for you. Real equity = long-term capital gains (~37.1% combined rate) vs phantom stock (~52.65% ordinary income). The tax delta on a $300M exit is massive.

**Your move:** Don't react with excitement yet. Ask clarifying questions:

1. "What type of equity — restricted stock, options, LLC units?"
2. "What's the strike price / valuation basis?"
3. "Is there a 409A or fair market value assessment?"
4. "Does it qualify for QSBS exclusion?" (could eliminate up to $10M in federal gains)

**Key math to have ready:**
- 3% real equity at $300M, taxed at ~37.1% = **$5.66M net** (vs $4.26M phantom)
- 2.5% real equity at $300M = **$4.72M net** — actually clears your $4M bar
- If QSBS applies: even better (up to $10M federal exclusion)

> *"That's interesting — I'd want to understand the details, but directionally that could actually work better for both of us. Lower percentage, same net outcome for me, and it costs the company less on a gross basis. Let me take a day to model it out."*

## Scenario D: Kevin counters low — 1.5-2% phantom

**What it sounds like:**

**What it means:** His advisors anchored him to a number that works in gross terms but doesn't clear your bar after tax. He likely hasn't internalized the 52.65% phantom tax hit.

**Your move:** Don't react negatively. Deploy the spreadsheet.

> *"I appreciate you sharing that. I know you've been thinking about this a lot. Can I pull up the model I sent you? I want to make sure we're looking at the same after-tax picture, because the phantom structure has a real tax cost that changes the math significantly."*

Walk through it:
- 2% phantom at $300M = **$2.84M net** (vs his mental math of "$6M")
- 2% phantom at $500M = **$4.74M net** (vs his "$10M")
- Then: "At 2%, I'd need a $500M+ exit just to hit the number we talked about. That's not impossible, but it means I'm betting on the upside scenario just to get to my floor."

**Then pivot to solutions, not complaints:**
- Option 1: Higher percentage phantom (3.5-4%)
- Option 2: Lower percentage real equity (2.5-3% real equity nets more than 3.5% phantom)
- Option 3: Tiered structure with performance kickers

> *"I'm not trying to make this hard. I want to find the structure that works for both of us. What if we..."*

## Scenario E: Kevin wants to restructure — not just comp

**What it sounds like:**
- "I've been thinking about the role differently"
- "My advisors had some thoughts on scope"
- "What if we started with a VP Ops title and moved to COO after 12 months"

**What it means:** Someone (advisor, board member, PE contact) challenged whether a first-time COO should get the full title + comp on day one. Kevin may still want you but is managing external pressure.

**Your move:** Separate title from comp. Title is negotiable; economics are not.

> *"I'm open to discussing how we phase the title — that's a fair conversation. But I want to make sure the economics reflect the scope of what I'm actually doing from day one. If I'm running operations, building the team, and driving toward exit — that's COO work regardless of what's on the business card."*

**Watch for the trap:** A "VP Ops" title at lower comp with a "path to COO" = you doing the same job for less money with no guarantee of the upgrade. If he goes here, the comp needs to be pegged to the full COO scope with a built-in step-up at the title change.

**What you're listening for:** Is this a process delay (fine) or a commitment delay (concerning)? If he can't articulate what's still outstanding, that's a signal.
