## Kevin's "Big Lever" Philosophy
<!-- id: sb-philosophy -->

<p>Kevin's clearest strategic insight — from the DTC Podcast — is that thinking like a business (not just a growth marketing business) unlocks disproportionate impact. The Costco example is his proof point: "a few emails and phone calls" took them from 0 to 15% of revenue "almost overnight."</p>

<div class="answer-block"><strong>Kevin's exact words:</strong> "A big lesson I've learned is that we really want to think about ourselves as a business, not just an e-commerce business or just a growth marketing business. There's so much more levers such as offline channels, such as retailers... pulling strategic levers so I can really drive that impact versus just tactical growth hack stuff all day."</div>

<p>The B2B/med spa play (see <a href="#b2b">Section 17</a>) is one such lever. Here are 5 more that follow the same pattern — massive impact from relatively focused effort.</p>

<div class="tip"><strong>Interview angle:</strong> Kevin should hear you say: "I think like you do — what's the one phone call, the one partnership, the one structural move that 10x's the result compared to incremental optimization? That's how I'd run operations for you."</div>

    <tr><th>Factor</th><th>Detail</th></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Why it works</strong></td><td>iRESTORE's FDA clearance + clinical data + 29,000+ reviews give them a stronger story than most beauty device competitors. One partnership could do for skincare what Costco did for hair.</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Potential impact</strong></td><td>0 → 10–15% of skincare revenue. Sephora has 2,700+ stores globally; Ulta has 1,400+ in the US.</td></tr>

    <tr><td><strong>Precedent</strong></td><td>NuFACE went from DTC to Sephora and it transformed their brand. CurrentBody's Nordstrom placement drove massive organic awareness.</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Exit impact</strong></td><td>Retail distribution in prestige beauty channels significantly expands the pool of strategic acquirers (L'Oréal, Shiseido, Estée Lauder all acquire brands with retail distribution).</td></tr>

## Bet 4: Subscription Consumables Flywheel
<!-- id: sb-subscriptions -->

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Monetizing the Existing Customer Base</div>
  <p>iRESTORE already sells shampoo, serum, conditioner, and supplements — but these appear to be treated as add-ons, not a strategic revenue pillar. With 600,000+ device buyers, this is an enormous untapped asset.</p>
  <table>
    <tr><th>Factor</th><th>Detail</th></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>The math</strong></td><td>600,000+ past device buyers. Even 10% conversion to $40/month subscription = <strong>$28.8M in recurring annual revenue</strong>. At 15% conversion = $43M.</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Why it works</strong></td><td>Device buyers already trust the brand. Post-purchase email flow → bundled offer → subscribe-and-save is a proven DTC playbook (Dollar Shave Club, Olaplex, Function of Beauty).</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Effort level</strong></td><td>Operational lift is small — build a post-purchase flow, create bundle pricing, add subscribe-and-save option. This is marketing + systems, not new products.</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Exit impact</strong></td><td><strong>This is the biggest exit-value lever on this list.</strong> Acquirers pay 2–4x higher multiples for subscription/recurring revenue vs. one-time device sales. $30M in recurring consumable revenue could add $100M+ to enterprise value.</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Strategic depth</strong></td><td>The subscription flywheel also improves LTV, reduces CAC dependency (you don't have to acquire the customer again), and provides predictable cash flow for planning.</td></tr>
  </table>
  <div class="tip"><strong>Kevin already mentioned this indirectly:</strong> He talked about consumables (shampoo, serum, supplements) as part of the rebrand to become a "beauty brand, not a device company." A subscription model is the operational engine that makes that vision real.</div>
</div>

## Bet 6: Tier-1 Celebrity Partnership (Kylie Jenner, Hailey Bieber, etc.)
<!-- id: sb-celebrity -->

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">One Face That Moves the Entire Market</div>
  <p>Kevin already uses influencer marketing (YouTube sponsorships, UGC whitelisting), but that's tactical. A tier-1 celebrity partnership — think Kylie Jenner, Hailey Bieber, or someone of that caliber — is a completely different lever. It's brand transformation in a single deal.</p>
  <table>
    <tr><th>Factor</th><th>Detail</th></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Why Kylie Jenner specifically</strong></td><td>She built Kylie Skin (sold to Coty for $600M). She already operates in beauty devices and skincare. Her audience (400M+ Instagram followers) skews heavily toward women 18–35 — exactly the demographic iRESTORE is trying to reach with the rebrand. A Kylie co-sign on LED face masks or the skincare wand would be seismic.</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Why it works for iRESTORE</strong></td><td>Kevin said 50% of customers are already women, and the rebrand is designed to connect emotionally, not technically. A tier-1 beauty celebrity does in one post what millions of ad spend can't — it makes iRESTORE a beauty brand, not a device company. This directly accelerates Kevin's stated vision.</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Potential impact</strong></td><td>Celebrity beauty device launches routinely generate $10–50M+ in initial demand. Therabody (Theragun) went from niche to mainstream through celebrity adoption. A Kylie or Hailey Bieber (Rhode Skin) partnership on LED masks could generate $20–40M in year-one incremental revenue.</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Deal structures</strong></td><td>Multiple options: (1) <strong>Brand ambassador</strong> — flat fee + royalty, $2–5M/yr for tier-1, (2) <strong>Co-branded product line</strong> — "Kylie x iRESTORE" LED mask at premium price point, revenue share, (3) <strong>Equity deal</strong> — give 1–3% equity for ongoing partnership, aligning incentives through the exit. Equity deals are increasingly common (Ryan Reynolds/Mint Mobile, George Clooney/Casamigos model).</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Effort level</strong></td><td>Higher than other bets — requires talent agency relationships (WME, CAA, UTA), negotiation, and a $2–5M+ commitment. But the ROI math is extraordinary if the celebrity's audience aligns.</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Exit impact</strong></td><td><strong>Massive.</strong> "Backed by Kylie Jenner" or "Hailey Bieber partnership" fundamentally changes how acquirers perceive the brand. It signals consumer relevance and staying power. L'Oréal, Estée Lauder, and Shiseido all pay premium multiples for brands with celebrity cachet.</td></tr>
