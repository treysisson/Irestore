## Closing Script: End of Interview
<!-- id: close-script -->

<h3>The Final 5 Minutes Matter Most</h3>
<p>How you close the interview signals your executive presence and genuine interest. Kevin will remember the ending more than the middle.</p>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">The Three-Part Close</div>
        <ol>
          <li><strong>Summarize your understanding</strong> (shows you listened):
            <div class="answer-block">"Kevin, here's what I'm hearing: you've built something incredible — $100M+ with a small team, dominant market position, and a product that actually works. The challenge now is that growth is outpacing your operational infrastructure. You need someone who can build the systems, manage the team, and free you to focus on innovation and vision. That's exactly what I do."</div>
          </li>
          <li><strong>Declare your interest</strong> (don't be coy):
            <div class="answer-block">"I want to be direct — I'm very excited about this opportunity. The combination of the growth stage, the EOS framework, the exit timeline, and the chance to build real operational infrastructure at a company that's already winning — that's rare. This is the kind of role I've been looking for."</div>
          </li>
          <li><strong>Ask the commitment question</strong> (creates next steps):
            <div class="answer-block">"What would you need to see from me to feel confident about moving forward? And what does the rest of the process look like from here?"</div>
          </li>
        </ol>
      </div>

## Handling Kevin's Concerns
<!-- id: close-objections -->

<h3>Likely Objections and How to Address Them</h3>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">"You don't have beauty/DTC experience"</div>
        <div class="answer-block">"That's fair — I haven't worked in beauty specifically. But what iRESTORE needs right now isn't another beauty marketer — it's operational infrastructure. Supply chain, EOS, fulfillment, finance, team management — those are industry-agnostic skills. And frankly, I think coming from outside the beauty bubble is an advantage. I'll ask 'why do we do it this way?' questions that an industry insider might not."</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">"You haven't managed a team this size"</div>
        <div class="answer-block">"The direct team under the COO here is 5–7 department heads — that's very manageable. The broader organization of 100+ people is about building systems, not managing individuals. My strength is building the operating system — the meeting rhythms, scorecards, accountability structures — that allows a team of 100 to function like a team of 20. That's what EOS is designed to do."</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">"We're worried about culture fit — we're scrappy"</div>
        <div class="answer-block">"I love that about iRESTORE. I'm not coming in to add bureaucracy — I'm coming in to add just enough structure that the scrappiness can scale. The best operators are player-coaches: I'll build the dashboards AND I'll be on the warehouse floor during a Q4 crunch. I don't believe in 'that's not my job.'"</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">"We've interviewed other candidates with more relevant experience"</div>
        <div class="answer-block">"I'd expect that — this is a great opportunity and you should have options. Here's what I'd ask you to weigh: do you want someone who's done this exact role before, or someone who brings fresh thinking and will build it the right way? The Q4 crisis happened because someone was doing things the way they'd always been done. I'm wired to ask 'what should this look like?' before 'how has it always been done?'"</div>
      </div>

## Post-Interview Follow-Up
<!-- id: close-followup -->

<h3>Within 24 Hours</h3>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Thank-you email to Kevin:</strong> Short, specific, personal. Reference something specific from the conversation that resonated. Don't just say "great meeting" — prove you were listening.</li>
        <li><strong>Bonus move:</strong> Send a brief (1-page max) "first impressions" document. Not a full 90-day plan — just 3–5 observations about the opportunity and how you'd approach the first 30 days. This signals executive initiative and gives Kevin something to share internally.</li>
      </ul>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">Follow-Up Email Template</div>
        <div class="answer-block">
          <p>Kevin,</p>
          <p>Thank you for the conversation today. I walked away even more excited about the opportunity.</p>
          <p>Three things stood out to me: [specific thing 1], [specific thing 2], and [specific thing 3]. These confirm my belief that the timing is right for an operational leader who can build the infrastructure to support your growth trajectory.</p>
          <p>I've attached a brief document with my initial observations — not a full plan, but a starting point for how I'd approach the first 30 days. I'd welcome your feedback.</p>
          <p>Looking forward to next steps.</p>
          <p>Best,<br>Trey</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="tip"><strong>The goal:</strong> Leave Kevin thinking "this person is already operating like they work here." The follow-up document is the single most differentiating thing you can do — most candidates send a thank-you email and wait. You send a deliverable.</div>

## Negotiation Sequencing
<!-- id: close-negotiate -->

<h3>After They Make an Offer</h3>
<p>Don't negotiate in the interview. Get the offer first, then negotiate from strength.</p>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">Sequencing</div>
        <ol>
          <li><strong>Receive offer → Express enthusiasm (but don't accept)</strong>: "I'm really excited about this. Thank you. I'd like to take 48 hours to review the details carefully — this is an important decision for both of us."</li>
          <li><strong>Review with your advisor / lawyer:</strong> Equity terms need legal review. Vesting, acceleration, dilution protection, valuation methodology — these matter enormously.</li>
          <li><strong>Counter on 2–3 points max:</strong> Don't nickel-and-dime. Pick the 2–3 things that matter most (likely equity % and acceleration clause) and make a clean counter.</li>
          <li><strong>Frame every ask as mutual benefit:</strong> "I want to be aligned with the company's success. An acceleration clause means I'm incentivized to drive the exit value as high as possible — that benefits both of us."</li>
        </ol>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">What to Negotiate (Priority Order)</div>
        <table>
          <tr><th>Item</th><th>Your Target</th><th>Rationale</th></tr>
          <tr><td><strong>Equity %</strong></td><td>5–8% (anchor at 8%)</td><td>See Compensation section — backed by 7 independent sources</td></tr>
          <tr><td><strong>Acceleration on exit</strong></td><td>Double-trigger acceleration</td><td>If company sells and you're terminated, unvested equity accelerates</td></tr>
          <tr><td><strong>Anti-dilution</strong></td><td>Weighted-average anti-dilution</td><td>Protects if they raise capital pre-exit</td></tr>
          <tr><td><strong>Base salary</strong></td><td>Market rate for LA-area COO</td><td>Don't let equity replace cash. Base should be livable without the exit.</td></tr>
          <tr><td><strong>Vesting schedule</strong></td><td>4-year with 1-year cliff</td><td>Standard. Push for monthly vesting after cliff (not quarterly).</td></tr>
          <tr><td><strong>Decision authority</strong></td><td>Written V/I accountability chart</td><td>Non-negotiable. Define authority before starting.</td></tr>
        </table>
      </div>
