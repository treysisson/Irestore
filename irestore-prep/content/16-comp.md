<h2>Why 5–8% Is Your Number</h2>
      <p>Seven independent sources — legal firms, venture advisors, executive recruiters, and academic research — converge on a range of <strong>3–8% for first COOs</strong> at growth-stage companies. You’d be the first C-suite hire at a pre-exit, bootstrapped company where the founder controls 100% of the cap table — building the operational engine from scratch. Your target should be the <strong>upper half of the first-COO range: 5–8%</strong>.</p>

## Phantom vs. Real Equity

<h2>Phantom Equity vs. Real Equity: Why This Matters Enormously</h2>
      <p>Kevin's JD says "equity" but his structure likely means <strong>phantom stock</strong>. This is the single most important negotiation point because phantom equity is taxed as <strong>ordinary income</strong> (~52.65% all-in in California) vs. real equity's <strong>long-term capital gains</strong> (~37.1%). That ~15.5-point difference costs you millions at exit.</p>
      <p class="muted"><strong>Tax breakdown — Real equity:</strong> 20% federal LTCG + 3.8% NIIT (Net Investment Income Tax) + 13.3% CA = <strong>37.1%</strong>. <strong>Phantom:</strong> 37% federal ordinary income + 2.35% Medicare + 13.3% CA = <strong>52.65%</strong>. <strong>QSBS (if eligible, 5yr hold):</strong> first $15M of gain excluded from federal tax → 13.3% CA only; above $15M at 37.1%. <strong>OBBBA tiered:</strong> 3yr hold = 50% exclusion (~30% eff.), 4yr = 75% (~26.4% eff.), 5yr = 100% (~22.8% eff.).</p>
      <p class="muted">Tax treatment sources: <a href="https://rsmus.com/insights/services/business-tax/9-frequently-asked-questions-about-phantom-stock-plans.html" target="_blank">RSM (formerly McGladrey)</a> confirms phantom payouts are taxed as ordinary income. <a href="https://carta.com/learn/startups/compensation/equity-incentive-plans/phantom-equity/" target="_blank">Carta's Phantom Equity Guide</a> details the structural differences. <a href="https://www.eisneramper.com/insights/employee-benefit-plan/section-409a-closely-held-business-1011/" target="_blank">EisnerAmper</a> covers 409A compliance requirements.</p>

## Must-Have Protections
<!-- id: comp-protection -->

<h2>Non-Negotiable Protections</h2>
      <p>These are the terms that protect your equity from being worth $0. Do not accept an offer without these, regardless of the percentage.</p>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">1. Double-Trigger Acceleration (CRITICAL)</div>
        <p><strong>What it means:</strong> If the company is acquired AND you are terminated (or role significantly changed) within 12–24 months of closing, 100% of your unvested equity vests immediately.</p>
        <p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> Without this, an acquirer can fire you on day 1 and your unvested equity disappears. At 3% with 2 years remaining on your vest, that's $7.5M at a $500M exit — gone.</p>
        <p><strong>What to say:</strong> "This is standard for C-level pre-exit hires. It protects us both — it ensures I stay motivated to maximize exit value rather than worrying about my own position post-close."</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">2. Anti-Dilution Protection (or Ratchet)</div>
        <p><strong>What it means:</strong> Your % doesn't get diluted below a floor if Kevin issues more equity to other executives or investors before exit.</p>
        <p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> Kevin could issue another 5% to a new VP of Sales, diluting your 3% to 2.85%. Over multiple hires, this adds up.</p>
        <p><strong>What to say:</strong> "I'd like a provision that my equity percentage isn't diluted below [X]% through future grants. If you need to issue more equity, I'd want a proportional top-up."</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">3. Clear Definition of "Change of Control"</div>
        <p>Must explicitly include: sale of >50% of assets, merger, acquisition, majority ownership change. Should NOT be limited to "100% sale" — partial acquisitions and PE deals should trigger your protections too.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">4. Section 409A Compliance (If Phantom)</div>
        <p>Phantom equity must be structured to comply with IRC Section 409A. Non-compliance triggers a <strong>20% excise tax penalty + interest</strong> on top of regular income tax. That could turn a 52% effective tax rate into 72%. Insist on legal review.</p>
        <p class="muted">Source: <a href="https://rsmus.com/insights/services/business-tax/9-frequently-asked-questions-about-phantom-stock-plans.html" target="_blank">RSM's Phantom Stock FAQ</a> and <a href="https://www.eisneramper.com/insights/employee-benefit-plan/section-409a-closely-held-business-1011/" target="_blank">EisnerAmper's 409A Guide</a>. Key: structure payouts as "short-term deferrals" (paid within 2.5 months of vesting) to qualify for the 409A exemption.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">5. Written Equity Terms in Offer Letter</div>
        <p>Percentage, type (real/phantom/hybrid), vesting schedule, acceleration triggers, payout mechanics, dilution protection — all in writing before you accept. "We'll figure it out later" = you have no equity.</p>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">6. Hire an Employment Lawyer</div>
        <p>Cost: $2K–$5K. This is non-negotiable for a role with $10M+ at stake. Focus areas: 409A compliance, acceleration language, change of control definition, payout mechanics, earnout terms. Kevin will expect this for a C-level hire.</p>

## How to Answer Comp Questions
<!-- id: comp-answer -->

<h2>Stage 1: The Interview Deflection (Before Any Offer)</h2>

<div class="warning"><strong>THE RULE:</strong> Do not name a number. Do not name a range. Do not even hint at a range. The person who names a number first anchors the negotiation — and almost always anchors it lower than they should. Your entire goal at this stage is to deflect gracefully while keeping the energy positive.</div>

<h3>Why Deflection Is the Right Strategy</h3>

<p>You’re in a strong negotiating position, and naming numbers now can only weaken it. Here’s why:</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">You Don’t Have Enough Information Yet</div>
  <p>You don’t know:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Whether Kevin is thinking real equity, phantom stock, profit interest units, or something else — and the tax treatment changes the math by 15–20 percentage points</li>
    <li>What the company’s actual valuation is (you’ve estimated, but Kevin may have a different number based on recent conversations with bankers or advisors)</li>
    <li>What the full package looks like — base, bonus structure, benefits, signing bonus, relocation support, etc.</li>
    <li>How much Kevin wants you after the onsite — your leverage increases with every successful interview stage</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Giving a number now is like bidding on a house before the inspection. You need to see the full picture first.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Your Leverage Increases With Every Stage</div>
  <p>Right now, Kevin is interviewing you. After the onsite — after he sees you build rapport with his team, walk the warehouse, and demonstrate strategic thinking — he’ll be <em>selling you</em>. That’s when you negotiate. Every interaction that goes well shifts the dynamic in your favor. Don’t negotiate from today’s leverage when tomorrow’s leverage is higher.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Kevin’s Incentive to Ask Early</div>
  <p>Kevin is a smart founder. If he asks about comp now, it’s because he wants to either: (a) screen you out if you’re way above budget, or (b) anchor you before you have full leverage. Both of these serve his interests, not yours. The deflection serves yours while keeping him comfortable that you’re not wildly misaligned.</p>
</div>

<h3>The Deflection Scripts</h3>

<p>Kevin will likely say something like: <em>"Last time we talked, you said you’d give some more thought to numbers and ranges — where’d you land?"</em> or <em>"What are your thoughts on comp?"</em></p>

<p>Here are three options — choose the one that matches Kevin’s energy in the moment. All three accomplish the same thing: no number given, but you demonstrate sophisticated thinking about comp <em>structure</em> and the variables that drive it. You’re showing you’ve done your homework — just not revealing your answer sheet.</p>

<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Option A: The "How I’m Thinking About It" Frame (Best Default — Shows Sophistication Without Numbers)</strong><br><br>
  "Yeah, I’ve been thinking about it a lot, actually. But honestly, the more I think about it, the more I realize the <em>structure</em> matters way more than any headline number — and there are a few things I’d need to understand before I could even put a number in context.<br><br>
  You mentioned last time that you’ve structured phantom stock for some directors and VPs, and you’d probably do something similar. I’ve been thinking a lot about that — specifically the difference between phantom and real equity, because the tax treatment changes the math enormously. We’re talking a 15-plus point spread between long-term capital gains and ordinary income in California. So my thinking on the percentage is going to be very different depending on which structure we’re in.<br><br>
  And then the other big variable is: what does the exit landscape actually look like? You mentioned a two-to-four year window with a target exit. But I’d want to understand — what’s the floor? What are you hoping for? What does a home run look like? Because that’s what turns an equity percentage into an actual number for both of us.<br><br>
  So I don’t want to throw out a number that doesn’t account for all of that. What I <em>can</em> tell you is that I’m not going to be unreasonable — I’m looking to be aligned with a big outcome, not to optimize for short-term cash. Does it make sense to have that deeper conversation once we’re both further along?"
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">🔧 Power Move: "I Think in Net Terms"</div>
  <p>You can weave this into <strong>any</strong> of the three options above. It’s a one-liner that does three things at once: forces the phantom-vs-real conversation, signals CFO-level sophistication, and positions you as someone who’s already done the math — without revealing what your actual number is.</p>

  <p><strong>The line:</strong></p>
  <p><em>"Honestly, I evaluate any equity package in after-tax terms, not headline terms. What matters to me is what the equity actually nets after California taxes — because that’s what determines whether the alignment is real or just on paper."</em></p>

  <p><strong>Variations you can drop naturally:</strong></p>
  <ul>
    <li><em>"I care more about what the equity nets me after California taxes than the headline percentage — and those two numbers can be very different depending on the structure."</em></li>
    <li><em>"At the end of the day, I’m thinking about this in net terms. The headline percentage matters less to me than what I actually take home — and in California, the structure drives that more than the number does."</em></li>
    <li><em>"One thing I’ve learned looking at this: the spread between phantom and real equity in California is massive after taxes. So when I think about what I need from the equity piece, I’m thinking in net terms — not gross."</em></li>
  </ul>

  <p><strong>Why this works:</strong></p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Forces the structure conversation.</strong> Kevin can’t respond to "I think in net terms" without engaging on the phantom-vs-real question. You’ve made it impossible to stay in headline-percentage land.</li>
    <li><strong>Signals you’ve done the math.</strong> Most candidates talk about percentages. You’re talking about after-tax outcomes. That’s a COO talking, not a candidate.</li>
    <li><strong>Doesn’t reveal your number.</strong> "I think in net terms" is a <em>framework</em>, not a target. Kevin doesn’t know if your net target is $5M or $20M — he just knows you’re sophisticated about it.</li>
    <li><strong>Creates an anchor without anchoring.</strong> By saying "net terms," you’ve implicitly said "your phantom offer will need to be higher than you think" — without naming how much higher.</li>
  </ul>

  <p><strong>⚠️ What NOT to say:</strong> Don’t share your actual net target number (e.g., "I need to net $15M"). That’s reverse-engineerable into a percentage at any given valuation, which is the same as naming a number. The power is in the <em>framework</em> — "I think in net terms" — not the specific figure.</p>
</div>

<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Option B: The "Variables That Drive My Thinking" Frame (Use If Kevin Pushes For More Specificity)</strong><br><br>
  "I hear you, and I want to be transparent about how I’m approaching this. I’ve done a ton of research — but the research keeps pointing me back to the same thing: the number is downstream of the structure.<br><br>
  For example — if we’re talking phantom stock, the all-in tax rate in California is north of 52%. If we’re talking real equity with LTCG treatment, it’s closer to 37%. That’s a 15-point gap that makes a massive difference at any exit valuation. So the same percentage means very different things depending on which bucket we’re in.<br><br>
  And then there are things about the <em>role itself</em> that I think should influence where in the range we land. This isn’t a maintenance hire — you’re not bringing me in to keep the trains running. You’re asking me to build the operational engine from scratch, drive margin improvement, stand up international, and prepare the company for an exit. I’d be the first C-suite hire, and I’d be functioning as a player-coach — not just managing but building. That scope is different from a typical COO slot, and I think the equity should reflect that.<br><br>
  So rather than give you a number right now, I’d rather understand a few things first: how you’re thinking about equity structure, what valuation range you’re modeling for an exit, and whether there’s flexibility on the phantom-versus-real question. With that context, I could give you something really specific. Without it, I’d just be guessing."
</div>

<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Option C: The "Return the Question With Context" Frame (Use If Kevin Is Very Direct)</strong><br><br>
  "I’d actually love to hear how your thinking has evolved since our first conversation. You mentioned phantom stock aligned to an exit — I’m curious whether you’ve gotten more specific on the valuation side. Like, what does a good outcome look like in your mind versus a great one?<br><br>
  Because for me, that’s the whole equation. The equity percentage only means something in the context of: what’s the exit target, what’s the structure, and what’s the timeline. I’ve been thinking about all of those variables, and I have a clear picture of what I’d need the package to look like — but I want to make sure my thinking is grounded in <em>your</em> numbers, not just my research.<br><br>
  What I will say is that I see this as a career-defining role. I’m not looking at this as a lateral move or a safe landing. You’re building something that could be worth a lot — and I want to be compensated in a way that means I’m all-in on making that happen. So my bias is heavily toward equity and alignment with the exit, not cash."
</div>

<h3>Handling Follow-Up Pressure</h3>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">If Kevin Says: "Just Give Me a Ballpark"</div>
  <p><strong>Don’t give a number.</strong> Instead, redirect to the variables:</p>
  <p><em>"I hear you. Here’s the honest answer: I can’t give you a meaningful ballpark because the equity structure changes the math too much. If we’re talking phantom stock — which is what you mentioned last time — that’s taxed as ordinary income, over 52% in California. If we’re talking actual equity with capital gains treatment, it’s 37%. Same percentage, wildly different outcomes. And then the valuation question — what you’d sell the company for, what a strong exit looks like versus a home run — that’s what turns a percentage into a real number. So rather than give you a meaningless ballpark, can I ask a couple of those structural questions? That’ll tell me more than any number I could throw out right now."</em></p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">If Kevin Says: "I Need to Know We’re in the Same Ballpark Before the Onsite"</div>
  <p>This is the strongest push — he’s gatekeeping. Acknowledge the concern, signal alignment, but stay on structure:</p>
  <p><em>"Totally fair — I don’t want either of us to waste time. Here’s what I can tell you: I’ve done my research on what first COOs earn at companies at your stage and scale, and my expectations are in line with market. I’m not going to come back with something wildly out of bounds. Where I want to spend time is on the structure — because whether the equity is phantom or real, what the vesting looks like, and what exit scenario we’re both building toward changes the total comp picture more than the base salary does. If that gives you enough comfort to keep going, I’d love to get to the onsite."</em></p>
  <p><strong>Why this works:</strong> You’ve confirmed you’re in the zone without revealing your number. And you’ve planted the seeds for the phantom-vs-real and valuation conversations you’ll need to have later.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">If Kevin Volunteers His Range First</div>
  <p>This is the ideal outcome. If Kevin says something like "We’re thinking $X base plus phantom equity," your response is:</p>
  <p><em>"Thanks for sharing that — I appreciate the transparency. I think there’s definitely a conversation to be had there. The piece I’d want to dig into is the equity structure. You mentioned phantom stock last time — I’ve been doing a lot of thinking about phantom versus real equity, and the tax math is pretty significant. I’d also want to understand what valuation you’re using for the phantom, and what exit range you’re modeling. Because that context changes how I think about the percentage entirely. Can we get into that when the time is right?"</em></p>
  <p><strong>What you’ve done:</strong> Acknowledged his number without accepting or rejecting it. Signaled that the equity conversation will be substantive. And planted the phantom-vs-real question early so it doesn’t blindside him later.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">If Kevin Asks: "What Questions Do You Have About the Structure?"</div>
  <p>This is a great outcome — he’s opened the door. Here are the questions to ask (these advance your negotiation without revealing your target):</p>
  <ul>
    <li><em>"Is the phantom stock program you mentioned valued at current company valuation, or is there a strike price / discount?"</em></li>
    <li><em>"When you think about an exit — what’s the floor you’d walk away from? What are you hoping for? What would a home run look like?"</em></li>
    <li><em>"Have you thought about whether real equity might make sense for this role, given the tax implications? I’ve been looking at the math and the difference is pretty material."</em></li>
    <li><em>"Is there flexibility on the equity type, or is phantom the only structure you’d consider?"</em></li>
    <li><em>"How are the existing phantom grants structured — change of control trigger only, or is there time-based vesting too?"</em></li>
  </ul>
  <p><strong>Why these questions matter:</strong> Each one gives you critical negotiation intel (valuation expectations, flexibility on structure, exit range) while signaling that you’re thinking about this at a level of sophistication that most candidates don’t. You’re having a CFO-level conversation about comp structure — which is exactly what a COO should do.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">What NOT to Do</div>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Don’t say "I’m flexible"</strong> — this signals you’ll take whatever they offer.</li>
    <li><strong>Don’t say "I haven’t thought about it too much" again</strong> — you said this last time. Saying it again signals you’re still not treating this seriously. Replace with "I’ve been thinking about it a lot, actually — specifically the structure."</li>
    <li><strong>Don’t cite your market data yet</strong> — save your Carnegie Mellon study, your seven sources, your SEC proxy comps for the actual negotiation. Showing your hand now reduces its impact later.</li>
    <li><strong>Don’t say a range</strong> — ranges are just numbers with extra steps. If you say "$300K–$400K," Kevin hears "$300K."</li>
    <li><strong>Don’t promise to "come back with numbers" again</strong> — you already said this once. This time, redirect: you’ll give specific numbers once you understand the structure.</li>
    <li><strong>Don’t apologize for not giving a number</strong> — this is a normal and expected move at the executive level. Kevin has done this himself in business negotiations.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tip"><strong>The meta-signal you’re sending:</strong> By deflecting gracefully, you’re actually demonstrating a COO skill — disciplined negotiation. Kevin is looking for someone who can negotiate vendor contracts, partner deals, and staffing terms on behalf of the company. How you handle your own comp negotiation is an audition for that. A COO who gives up leverage easily in their own negotiation will do the same with Costco’s buyer or a 3PL contract.</div>

## Scripts for the Offer-Stage Negotiation

<h2>Stage 2: When You Actually Negotiate (Post-Offer)</h2>

<p>These scripts are for after the onsite, when Kevin (or his recruiter) presents a formal or informal offer and you shift into actual negotiation. <strong>Do not use these during the interview stage.</strong></p>

      <h3>"What are you looking for in terms of compensation?"</h3>
      <div class="answer-block">
        <strong>At the offer stage (after onsite, when Kevin is ready to talk numbers):</strong><br>
        "I’ve done market research on COO compensation at $90–120M DTC companies in Southern California. I’m in the range of $350K–$400K base with a 40–50% performance bonus. On equity, I’d want to understand your structure first — whether you’re thinking real equity, phantom stock, or something else — because the tax treatment significantly affects how I think about percentages. What I care most about is that the equity is structured so I’m genuinely aligned with a big exit outcome. Can you share how you’re thinking about it?"
      </div>

      <div class="answer-block">
        <strong>When Kevin names 1–2% phantom:</strong><br>
        "I appreciate you sharing that. Let me be transparent about my thinking. I’ve modeled this out: at your target exit range of $500M+, 2% phantom nets me about $4.7M after California taxes — because phantom is taxed as ordinary income at roughly 53%. That’s meaningful, but for a role where I’m building the operational engine from scratch and directly driving exit value, I’d want my equity to net me in the $10–15M range at your target outcome.<br><br>
        Seven independent sources put first-COO equity at 3–8%. Carnegie Mellon’s Swartz Center puts the median at 5%. I’m asking for 5–6%, which is the middle-to-upper half of the sourced range. I know that’s above your initial number. But here’s the value exchange: the margin improvement alone in your scorecard — 3–5 points on $120M — creates $54–90M in enterprise value at a 15x multiple. You’d be paying 3% for someone who delivers 10–18% of incremental value. What if we explored a structure where part of it is performance-vested so you’re only paying the premium if I deliver?"
      </div>

      <div class="answer-block">
        <strong>If Kevin says "let me think about it":</strong><br>
        "Absolutely. I’d suggest we both take a few days. I can send you a one-pager showing the math — the tax treatment comparison, the value-creation math, and a few structural options that might work. I want us both to feel great about this."
      </div>

      <h3>"Why should I give you more than 1–2%?"</h3>
      <div class="answer-block">
        <strong>The value-creation argument:</strong><br>
        "Three reasons. First, the JD scorecard: 3–5 points of gross margin improvement at your revenue = $54–90M in enterprise value at exit. My equity ask is 3% — I’m creating 3–6x what I cost in equity alone. Second, I’m not a maintenance hire — I’m building the ops function from scratch, which is closer to a founding role than a hired-gun role. Founding COOs get 3–8%. Third, timing: I have a compressed 2–4 year window before exit. A typical COO has a 7–10 year horizon to benefit from equity. My per-year equity value needs to be higher to compensate for the shorter runway."
      </div>

      <div class="tip"><strong>Tone check:</strong> Never frame this as adversarial. You’re both trying to solve the same problem: how to structure comp so you’re fully motivated to maximize exit value. Kevin wins when you win. Keep it collaborative.</div>
