<h4>How to Read This — The Story It Tells</h4>
<div class="card card-green">
<h3>🎯 What Should Jump Out Immediately</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>DTC is the margin engine at 47% contribution margin.</strong> This is the channel you protect and grow. Every dollar shifted from Amazon to DTC is worth ~40 cents more in contribution.</li>
<li><strong>Amazon at 7.5% contribution margin looks terrible — but that's not the whole story.</strong> Kevin himself called Amazon a "research platform." <li><strong>Costco at 37% CM looks surprisingly good — but that 30% return rate is a time bomb.</strong> The lifetime return rate means units keep coming back for months/years. Today's 37% CM could be 20% once all returns settle. Ask: "Is this return rate fully seasoned, or are there units still in the return window?"</li>
<li><strong>B2B is tiny but high-margin.</strong> This validates Kevin's bet on the B2B division. At scale, B2B could be a 40-50% CM channel with lower return rates.</li>

<h4>Sub-Scenario A: "Should We Pull Back on Amazon?"</h4>
<div class="card">
<p><strong>Kevin might test your thinking:</strong> "Amazon is 33% of revenue but only 7% CM. Should we pull back?"</p>

<p><strong>Your answer: "No — but we need to be surgical about what we sell there."</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Amazon's role is customer acquisition, not margin.</strong> It's the discovery engine. Customers Google "hair growth device," land on Amazon, read reviews, then many go to iRESTORE.com for the 365-day trial and better bundles.</li>
<li><strong>The fix isn't pulling back — it's optimizing the Amazon P&L:</strong>
<ul>
<li>Shift low-ASP SKUs off Amazon (consumables with thin margins after FBA fees)</li>
<li>Focus Amazon on hero devices + bundles where the absolute $ CM justifies the fees</li>
<li>Move repeat/refill purchases to DTC via post-purchase flows ("Subscribe on iRESTORE.com, save 15%")</li>
<li>Test Seller Fulfilled Prime for oversized items (body panels) to avoid FBA fees while keeping Prime badge</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong>The real question:</strong> "What's the blended CAC when you attribute a portion of Amazon's awareness to DTC conversion? If Amazon is driving even $5M of DTC revenue that would otherwise cost $1.5M in paid media, then Amazon's true contribution is much higher."</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h4>Sub-Scenario B: "Costco Wants to Double Our Order. Should We?"</h4>
<div class="card">
<p><strong>This is a trap question.</strong> The obvious answer is yes (more revenue!). The right answer is conditional.</p>

<p><strong>Your answer: "Only if we can solve the return rate problem first."</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>At 30% lifetime return rate, every $1M in Costco revenue generates $300K in returns — and those returns have processing costs, refurb costs, and potential channel contamination (refurbs competing with new units)</li>
<li>Costco's return policy is famously generous — unlimited timeline, no questions asked. You can't change Costco's policy, but you can:
<ul>
<li>Design a Costco-specific SKU with scaled-back features at a lower price point (Kevin already does this) — ensure the margin absorbs the return rate</li>
<li>Include better onboarding materials in the Costco packaging</li>
<li>Add a "call us before returning" card with a direct line to a retention specialist</li>
</ul></li>
<li><strong>The math to present:</strong> "Costco doubles from $10M to $20M gross. At 30% returns, that's $6M in returned product. At 50% salvage rate, $3M is total loss. Net contribution after channel costs: maybe $2-3M. Is that the best use of $20M of manufacturing capacity and warehouse space when DTC generates 47% CM?"</li>
</ul>
</div>
