## Background
<!-- id: kevin-bg -->

<p>Kevin Chen is the co-founder and CEO of iRESTORE (Freedom Laser Therapy, Inc.). He co-founded the company with his father approximately 13 years ago. Originally from Taiwan, Kevin moved to Irvine, California at age 10. He attended UC Berkeley and launched the business at around age 25. He lives in Brentwood, LA, and commutes to the La Mirada office regularly.</p>
      <p>His father owns the manufacturing facility in Shenzhen, China, which gives iRESTORE a significant structural advantage in vertical integration and cost control.</p>

<h3>Personal Details (March 2026)</h3>
<p><strong>Age & Interests:</strong> Just turned 35 (birthday March 10, 2026 — celebrated in Vegas). Serious poker player — prefers cash games at Aria and Wynn, tracks results carefully as a winning player. Considering WSOP main event ($10K entry, 7-day commitment) as a bucket list item.</p>

<p><strong>Information Sources:</strong> Member of DDC (Direct-to-Consumer) mastermind with nine-figure brands — this is his primary information source for e-commerce strategy. It's private and not a paid membership. Recently attended Vegas e-commerce and Amazon trade show but found the sessions "terrible" — said his best insights come from private conversations with peers in the mastermind rather than public sessions.</p>

## Kevin's Origin Story & Company History
<!-- id: kevin-origin -->

<p>Kevin Chen co-founded iRESTORE with his father, who had previously run Freedom Laser Therapy — a clinic using laser therapy to treat nicotine addiction. When Kevin's father experienced hair loss and tried clinical laser therapy (expensive at $1000s, inconvenient with 3x/week visits), they saw the opportunity: bring clinical-grade laser therapy to home use.</p>

## Kevin's Scaling & Hiring Philosophy
<!-- id: kevin-scaling -->

<p>Kevin has a sophisticated, experience-tested framework for how to build and scale a team. This came through most clearly in his Money Talks interview and is directly relevant to the COO role.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">"Fire Yourself Every Year"</div>
  <p>Kevin shared a framework from a friend that he's adopted: <em>"Basically I just fire myself every year. Whatever he does, he reviews it and he's like — okay, I hire someone for that role. What I did last year, next year — what did I do? And then that kind of compounds."</em></p>
  <p>This is how Kevin has progressively moved from doing everything → delegating CS/ops → delegating growth marketing → now focusing almost exclusively on product R&D and recruiting. The COO role is the next major "fire yourself" move.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Value Creation vs. Value Protection</div>
  <p>Kevin categorizes all roles into two buckets:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Value creation:</strong> Product innovation, growth marketing — roles that drive growth</li>
    <li><strong>Value protection:</strong> Operations, logistics, customer service — roles that "keep the wheels on the car"</li>
  </ul>
  <p>His delegation strategy: <em>"You want to hold on for as long as you can to the value-driving things while doing a minimal set of value protection type of tasks. And then delegate that way."</em> He delegates value protection first, holds value creation as long as possible, then hires executives when scale demands it.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Pay the Premium — But Only for High-Leverage Roles</div>
  <p>Kevin endorses the "10x engineer" concept but with an important caveat about role leverage:</p>
  <div class="answer-block">
    "It comes down to whether that role has enough leverage to become 10x. Taking a simple example of a warehouse worker — the best warehouse worker is maybe 50% faster than an average warehouse worker. So you don't want to overpay there. But then take the growth marketer — especially today with AI — they can probably 20x the next person... So I want to make sure to think through how much leverage the role has." <span class="muted">— Money Talks</span>
  </div>
  <p><strong>Why this matters for you:</strong> The COO/Integrator role is the ultimate high-leverage position at iRESTORE right now. Kevin understands this — he's not looking for a bargain hire. Frame your compensation expectations around the leverage you'll create.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Hire People Better Than You</div>
  <div class="answer-block">
    "At a certain scale, you really have to hire the people that have been there, done that, and are better than you so you can truly let go. I've brought on people where I felt like I was better than them at their job. And what that meant was I was always hovering over their shoulders, looking over everything. It's not empowering. It's not good for them." <span class="muted">— Money Talks</span>
  </div>
  <p>Kevin has explicitly learned this lesson the hard way. He wants a COO who is clearly more experienced in operations than he is — someone he can trust completely.</p>
</div>

<h3>Kevin's Executive Coaching Investment</h3>

<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>On paying for knowledge:</strong> "Paying for knowledge at a certain scale is one of the most impactful things and highest leverage things you can do. We go back to the same framework — if my time is worth $1,000 an hour and I'm paying a coach $5,000 a month, well, if they can help me make one really good decision that leads to a six-figure outcome, it would be foolish not to pay for that." <span class="muted">— Money Talks</span>
</div>

<p>Kevin currently works with an executive coach who has P&G and beauty industry experience. This coach helped him understand retail channel strategy (the Costco SKU lesson that "would have saved six figures"). Kevin values <em>been-there-done-that</em> expertise — not theoretical advice. Your 15+ years of operational experience is exactly this kind of GPS-navigation for him.</p>

<div class="tip"><strong>Interview angle:</strong> Kevin's scaling philosophy perfectly describes the COO hire. He's delegated value protection (ops) to Brandon, but now needs someone who can do both — protect value AND create it through systems, process, and strategic operations. Position yourself as the person who lets him fully "fire himself" from operational thinking.</div>
