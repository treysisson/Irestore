## Q1: Operational Overwhelm
**Question:** "Tell me about a time your team got completely overwhelmed operationally — demand way outstripped your capacity. What happened, and what did you do?"

### Trey's Answer:
The best example is the small jewelry brand I worked with for over a year and a half. They'd been doing their own fulfillment out of an office/mini warehouse in Hawaii — nightmare for shipping costs, logistics, etc. Part of what I helped them with was re-merchandising and cutting down SKUs. We ran a massive liquidation which created a similar issue to what you experienced with Costco — 10x+ normal volume, completely overwhelmed. At one point they were maybe three weeks behind on orders.

During the crisis: extended team hours (very small team, maybe 2-3 warehouse workers). Incentivized with overtime plus a bonus. Asked them to recruit friends and family — went from 3 to 8-10 people. One founder flew to Hawaii for the duration. I coached him through improving the process — batching orders at certain times of day, carrier pickup cutoffs, multiple carrier pickups, staging environment with different stations, daily checkpoints tracking orders shipped (almost like a physical dashboard). Cut backlog from 2-3 weeks to consistently under 3-4 days.

Post-crisis: immediately started planning move to a 3PL. I helped identify options. The transition was complicated — Hawaii means no trucking, had to use boats and planes. Timed it carefully to avoid any gap in shipping capability.

### Coaching Notes:
- **Strong:** Real story with specifics. The friend/family recruiting mirrors exactly what Kevin wished Brandon had done in the Costco crisis. 3PL transition shows systemic thinking.
- **Improve:** Lead with framework first ("My first move is always identifying the constraint — labor, space, or process?"), then use the story as proof. Bridge explicitly to iRESTORE ("For a company at your scale with Costco's SLA requirements, I'd want that contingency infrastructure built before Q4"). Trim the Hawaii shipping logistics detail — one sentence is enough. Lean into the "I coached him" framing — that's fractional COO work.

## Q2: First 90 Days
**Question:** "Walk me through your first 90 days — what are you actually doing?"

### Trey's Answer:
Three chunks. First 30 days: listening, observing, in all the meetings, asking questions, building rapport with direct reports and team. Spending a ton of time with Kevin — more than 1-2 days/week in LA. Second month: we've identified how the org is working, develop a running list of easy wins. Put everything on a 2x2 grid (easy/difficult × low/high impact). Target the easy + high impact changes for quick wins. Last 30 days: refining the roadmap — product roadmap, internationalization, 3PL decisions, etc. Establishing the strategy and execution plan going forward.

Last thing: within 60-90 days, start offloading Kevin's day-to-day operational involvement. Not jumping in week 1-2 to run meetings — that'd be abrupt. Around day 30, start stepping in so Kevin can step back. Interesting AI/communication reporting opportunities so Kevin stays informed without being in every meeting.

### Coaching Notes:
- **Strong:** Clean 30/60/90 framework. 2x2 prioritization grid is concrete. Ending with "offloading Kevin" speaks directly to his pain. AI reporting is a differentiator.
- **Improve:** Add one specific thing you'd look at in Month 1 (e.g., "sit in on a full order cycle from PO to shipment and map the handoffs"). Give a concrete quick-win example ("there's always a vendor contract that hasn't been rebid in two years"). Make the Kevin offload more specific ("By Day 30 I'm running the weekly ops standup. By Day 60, Brandon comes to me first, and you're getting a weekly summary instead of sitting in five meetings"). Mention EOS — "I'd want to get into your EOS rhythm quickly — L10s, scorecard, Rocks — so I'm managing through your existing operating system, not reinventing one."

## Q6: Working with Brandon
**Question:** "We've got Brandon running ops today. How would you think about working with him? He's been here a while."

### Trey's Answer:
Same approach as any new employee — listen and understand in the first few weeks. Ask tons of questions, understand his day-to-day, who he works with, how, what his bottlenecks are. Also what his ideas are for improving the business and his own role, where he sees his role developing. Get his perspective and Kevin's. Download as much as possible, but approach with a critical eye — looking for opportunities where he can improve, stretch, change workflows, make the org better.

### Coaching Notes:
- **Decent foundation but needs more depth.** This is a loaded question — Brandon's limitations created Kevin's need for a COO.
- **Missing the subtext.** Acknowledge it: "I know part of the reason this role exists is to bring a different level of operational thinking. Brandon's been here, he knows the business, he knows the people. That's valuable and I wouldn't want to lose it."
- **Missing Month 3.** Kevin wants to know what happens after listening. Give two paths: "Either Brandon is a strong executor who's been stuck in reactive mode because nobody was setting the framework — in which case I'm coaching him up into a strong Director of Ops under me. Or the role has outgrown him and we have an honest conversation. But I'd want to earn the right to make that call by working alongside him first."
- **"Critical eye" is cold.** Reframe: "I'd want to understand what he's great at — because there's always something. And I want him to know early I'm not here to replace him, I'm here to make the whole team better."
- **Name the human dynamics:** "A new COO showing up above someone running ops is going to create anxiety. I'd have a direct conversation first week — here's my role, here's how I see us working together, no ambiguity."

### Refined Answer (in Trey's voice):
"Yeah, so first off — I understand the dynamic here. Part of the reason this role exists is to bring a different level of operational thinking to the business. And Brandon's been here, he knows the business inside and out, he knows the people, the vendors, the day-to-day. That's genuinely valuable and I wouldn't want to lose it.

So my approach would be the same as any new team member — spend the first few weeks really downloading from him. What's his day-to-day look like, what are his bottlenecks, who does he work with, how does information flow through his team. But I'd also want to understand his perspective on where the gaps are — because people in the trenches usually know exactly what's broken, they just haven't had the support or authority to fix it. So I want to hear his ideas too.

After about 30 days, I'll have a pretty clear picture. And it's going to go one of two ways. Either Brandon is a strong executor who's been stuck in reactive mode because there hasn't been anyone above him setting the framework — the capacity planning, the scenario modeling, the SOPs — and in that case, I'm coaching him up and he becomes a really strong Director of Ops under me. Or, the role has outgrown where he is, and we have an honest conversation about what's next. But I'd want to earn the right to make that call by actually working alongside him first.

The one thing I'd do really early — probably first week — is have a direct conversation with him. No ambiguity. Here's my role, here's how I see us working together, here's what I need from you, and here's how I'm going to support you. Because I'm realistic — a new COO showing up above someone who's been running ops is going to create some anxiety. I'd rather name that and get ahead of it than let it fester."
