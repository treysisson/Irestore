## Big Lever Stories (Disproportionate-Impact Moves)

<p>Kevin's entire strategic philosophy is "what's the one phone call, one partnership, one structural move that 10x's the result?" Here are three times you've done exactly that.</p>

<div class="card">

  <p><strong>The situation:</strong> Stay Golden was massively over-SKU'd. They had an enormous long tail of jewelry products — too many styles, too many variations — that was destroying margins and operational efficiency.</p>
  <p><strong>What you did:</strong> Identified the long-tail problem through data analysis. Ran strategic liquidation and clearance sales to move through slow-moving inventory.   <div class="card-header">B2TM: From Monthly Drops to Strategic Drops</div>
  <p><strong>The situation:</strong> B2TM was doing frequent drops — almost monthly — which kept the brand buzzy but was operationally unsustainable. Each drop required a full cycle of product development, production coordination, marketing, and fulfillment. The pace was grinding down the small team.</p>
  <p><strong>What you did:</strong> Shifted the cadence to fewer, more strategic drops. Instead of monthly, you focused on bigger, more impactful launches spaced further apart. Used sell-outs as a marketing engine — when products sold out, it created urgency and FOMO that drove demand for the next drop. Applied more financial discipline to each drop decision: better quantity planning, smarter style selection, stronger margin analysis.</p>
  <p><strong>The impact:</strong> Better financial performance per drop (fewer bets, but each one more calculated). Reduced operational strain on the team. And counterintuitively, the scarcity created by fewer drops actually <em>improved</em> brand heat — customers paid more attention to each launch because they were events, not just another Tuesday.</p>
  <p><strong>Why it's a "big lever":</strong> Sometimes the big lever is doing <em>less</em> — but doing it better. This is directly relevant to iRESTORE's product pipeline. With skincare devices, professional panels, the sub-$200 wand, and consumables all in development, the temptation is to launch everything at once. A COO who can say "let's sequence these strategically" is exactly what Kevin needs.</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Hollow Socks / Easy Street: Getting Ahead of Raw Material Purchasing</div>
  <p><strong>The situation:</strong> Hollow Socks uses alpaca yarn from Peru — a specialty material with limited supply and long lead times. The business was growing fast (headed from $20M toward $70–80M), and if they didn't lock down raw materials ahead of demand, they'd hit a ceiling where growth stalls because you literally can't get enough yarn.</p>
  <p><strong>What you did:</strong> Got ahead of the purchasing cycle at the <em>raw material</em> level — not just finished goods. Worked directly with Peru-based fiber suppliers to negotiate volume commitments. Had the factories store the yarn on-site so it was ready for production as orders came in. This meant the supply chain was pre-loaded with capacity months before the sales materialized.</p>
  <p><strong>The impact:</strong> Enabled the growth trajectory from $20M to a projected $70–80M without supply chain bottlenecks. Without this move, growth would have stalled — you can't sell socks you can't make, and you can't make socks without yarn. This was the foundational supply chain decision that made everything else possible.</p>
  <p><strong>Why it's a "big lever":</strong> This is strategic operations at its purest — one structural supply chain decision that unlocked $50–60M in growth. The iRESTORE parallel is obvious: as Kevin scales into new product categories and new geographies, the supply chain needs to be <em>ahead</em> of demand, not reacting to it. This story proves you think that way.</p>
</div>

<br><br>
<strong>If he's talking about product strategy:</strong> Tell the B2TM drop cadence story — "Sometimes the big lever is doing less, but better."
<br><br>
<strong>If he's talking about supply chain or scaling:</strong> Tell the Hollow Socks yarn story — "I've made the structural supply chain moves that unlock the next stage of growth."
<br><br>
<strong>If he's talking about margins, efficiency, or cleaning up the business:</strong> Tell the Stay Golden inventory story — "One strategic analysis of the product line freed up cash, improved margins, and simplified operations."
<br><br>
The key message across all three: "I look for the one structural move that creates disproportionate impact. That's how I think — and it's exactly how you described thinking about the Costco deal."</div>
