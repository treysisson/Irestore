## Kevin's Wholesale Lesson (Money Talks Podcast)
<!-- id: ws-kevin-lesson -->

<div class="tip"><strong>Context:</strong> Kevin specifically discusses wholesale/retail channel strategy in the Money Talks podcast (~42:00 mark). His P&G beauty coach taught him channel pricing — knowledge that "saved six figures." This is one of Kevin's clearest operational pain points and a direct opportunity for the COO.</div>

<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Kevin's exact words:</strong> "When you go into a Costco, when you go into a retailer, you need to think about... Costco takes 12% margin. For example, Walmart and Target, they start at 40% gross margin. So when you're designing your product, you need to have that margin, right? The second thing is that you need to think about your channel pricing strategy. You don't just set a price for your product. You think about in this channel, it's this price. In this other channel, it's this price."
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Kevin's Channel Pricing Mistakes (His Own Words)</div>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Mistake 1 — No SMU strategy:</strong> Put the same product in Costco as DTC/Amazon. Costco priced it cheaper → cannibalized DTC sales. People found it "$200–300 cheaper" on Costco and stopped buying on the website.</li>
    <li><strong>Mistake 2 — No channel-specific product design:</strong> "Had I known that and like thoughtfully designed what the assortment strategy needs to be in different retailers" — he admits he didn't plan product variants per channel.</li>
    <li><strong>The fix (from his coach):</strong> Create <strong>SMU (Special Make-Up)</strong> products — exclusive SKUs per channel. "Maybe it's different color, maybe it's different whatever but inherently a different product and so you're not in price competition."</li>
    <li><strong>Cost of the lesson:</strong> "That would have saved us six figures" — Kevin explicitly quantifies the cost of not having this knowledge upfront.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Your angle:</strong> "I heard you talk about the SMU lesson on Money Talks — that's exactly the kind of operational architecture I build. Channel pricing strategy, MAP enforcement, exclusive SKU planning — these aren't afterthoughts, they're day-one infrastructure for any retail expansion. I'd make sure we never leave six figures on the table like that again."
</div>

## Key Concepts for the Interview
<!-- id: ws-concepts -->

<h3>MAP Policy (Minimum Advertised Price)</h3>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">MAP — The Foundation of Multi-Channel Pricing</div>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>What it is:</strong> A manufacturer's rule setting the lowest price a reseller can <em>advertise</em> publicly. Does NOT restrict the actual selling price (in-cart/checkout pricing can be lower). This is a legal distinction — outright price-fixing is illegal, but MAP is generally enforceable.</li>
    <li><strong>Why iRESTORE needs it:</strong> Selling through DTC, Amazon, Costco, and potentially Sephora/Ulta/Target creates price competition across channels. Without MAP, a retailer could advertise the Elite at $499 (instead of $695), tanking perceived value everywhere.</li>
    <li><strong>Internet MAP (iMAP):</strong> Separate minimum prices for online channels to account for Amazon/marketplace dynamics.</li>
    <li><strong>Enforcement:</strong> Requires automated monitoring across Amazon, Costco.com, Target.com, Sephora.com, marketplace sellers. Violation penalties: supply restrictions, account suspension. Tools cost $2-5K/month.</li>
    <li><strong>COO first-week action:</strong> MAP policy must be finalized with legal/finance before any new retail launch. Non-negotiable.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<h3>SMU (Special Make-Up) Products</h3>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">SMU — Kevin's "Six-Figure Lesson"</div>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>What it is:</strong> Custom/exclusive product variants made specifically for one retailer. Different color, packaging, bundle, or specification — "inherently a different product" so you're not in direct price competition across channels.</li>
    <li><strong>Kevin's example:</strong> "That's why when you go in Costco you see these different pack sizes and stuff — a 24-pack instead of a 12-pack of soda, or a bundle of three for Tide instead of one in Target."</li>
    <li><strong>For iRESTORE:</strong> Costco gets exclusive color. Amazon gets device + supplement bundle. Sephora gets device + serum kit. DTC gets the "standard" SKU at full MSRP. No cross-channel price comparison possible.</li>
    <li><strong>Cost trade-off:</strong> SMU adds ~$2/unit in COGS (custom color/packaging) but saves six figures in margin erosion from channel conflict. Easy math.</li>
    <li><strong>Operational complexity:</strong> Separate SKU tracking, separate COGS, separate MOQs per channel. Requires strong inventory management — this is a COO infrastructure build.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

## Exit Implications of Channel Diversification
<!-- id: ws-exit -->

<div class="tip"><strong>Core logic:</strong> More retail channels = more types of acquirers who want you. Getting INTO Sephora/Ulta expands the buyer pool. Staying out limits it.</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Why Multi-Channel Matters for the $500M Exit</div>
  <table>
    <tr><th>Current State (Without Retail)</th><th>Target State (With Retail)</th><th>Impact on Valuation</th></tr>
    <tr><td>DTC + Amazon + Costco only = ~95% of revenue</td><td>DTC + Amazon + Costco + 3-4 retail channels = balanced mix</td><td>Reduces Amazon concentration risk → lower buyer risk → <strong>higher multiple</strong></td></tr>
    <tr><td>No prestige retail → acquirer pool limited to PE firms and DTC roll-ups</td><td>Sephora/Ulta shelf presence → <strong>opens the door to strategic beauty acquirers</strong> (L'Oréal, Shiseido, Estée Lauder, P&G)</td><td><strong>Larger acquirer pool = competitive bidding = higher price.</strong> Strategic buyers pay 6-8x vs. PE's 3-5x.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>~65% gross margin (DTC-heavy)</td><td>~50% blended gross margin (retail dilution)</td><td>Margin compression is the acceptable trade-off. Acquirers pay for diversified revenue, not just margin.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>No retail ops infrastructure</td><td>EDI, MAP, SMU strategy, retail fulfillment SOP all built</td><td>Shows operational maturity → acquirer sees scalable platform, not founder dependency</td></tr>
  </table>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Proof Point: Beauty Brands That Expanded Acquirer Pool via Retail</div>
  <table>
    <tr><th>Brand</th><th>Retail Presence</th><th>Acquirer</th><th>Exit Value</th></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Drunk Elephant</strong></td><td>Sephora exclusive → then expanded</td><td>Shiseido</td><td>$845M</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>Tatcha</strong></td><td>Sephora</td><td>Unilever</td><td>~$500M</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>NuFACE</strong></td><td>DTC → Sephora (transformed brand)</td><td>Strategic (PE-backed growth)</td><td>Significant multiple uplift</td></tr>
    <tr><td><strong>FOREO</strong></td><td>Sephora, Ulta, Nordstrom</td><td>Multiple strategic offers</td><td>$1B+ valuation</td></tr>
  </table>
  <p class="muted">Pattern: Every major beauty device/skincare exit that commanded a premium multiple had prestige retail distribution. DTC-only brands sell for less.</p>
</div>

<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Exit framing:</strong> "A DTC + Amazon business at $90M revenue gets a 3-4x EBITDA multiple from PE buyers. A multi-channel brand with Costco, Sephora, and Target distribution — plus recurring consumable revenue — attracts strategic beauty conglomerates willing to pay 6-8x. Drunk Elephant got $845M from Shiseido, Tatcha got $500M from Unilever — both had Sephora distribution. The channel strategy isn't just about revenue growth. It's about building the kind of business that L'Oréal or Estée Lauder wants to buy."
</div>
