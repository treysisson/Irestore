## Red Flags to Watch For
<!-- id: risks-flags -->

<p>Every role has risks. Being aware of them doesn't mean you shouldn't take the job — it means you go in with eyes open and a plan for each one.</p>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">🚩 Founder Dependency & Decision Authority</div>
        <p><strong>The risk:</strong> Kevin is the CEO, the product visionary, the final interviewer for every hire, heavily involved in PD, and still making operational decisions. The title is "COO" but the actual authority may be limited if Kevin can't let go.</p>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Signal to watch:</strong> Does Kevin describe the role as "run the company" or "help me with the company"? The first implies real authority; the second implies glorified chief of staff.</li>
          <li><strong>Mitigant:</strong> In EOS, the Visionary/Integrator contract explicitly defines who owns what. Ask Kevin: "Are you willing to commit to the V/I accountability chart where I own all day-to-day operations?" If he hesitates, that's data.</li>
          <li><strong>Question to ask:</strong> "What decisions will I be able to make without checking with you? What's the threshold?"</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">🚩 Growth Rate vs. Infrastructure</div>
        <p><strong>The risk:</strong> 80% YoY growth with ~60% EOS implementation means the company is scaling faster than its systems. You'd be building the plane while flying it at Mach 2. The Q4 warehouse crisis is a symptom — there will be more.</p>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Signal to watch:</strong> How many fires are they fighting right now? If the interview process feels chaotic, imagine the daily ops.</li>
          <li><strong>Mitigant:</strong> This is actually the opportunity. The gap between growth and infrastructure is exactly why they need a COO. But be realistic about the first 6 months — it will be intense.</li>
          <li><strong>Question to ask:</strong> "What's the biggest operational fire you're dealing with right now that I should know about?"</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">🚩 Team Resistance to First COO</div>
        <p><strong>The risk:</strong> The team has been reporting directly to Kevin for years. A new COO layer means they lose direct access to the CEO. Brandon in particular — he's been the de facto ops leader. You'd be his new boss.</p>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Signal to watch:</strong> If you meet the team during the interview, notice who seems welcoming vs. guarded. Brandon's body language matters.</li>
          <li><strong>Mitigant:</strong> Lead with humility. "I'm not here to change everything. I'm here to learn from you first." Build relationships in Days 1–30 before making structural changes.</li>
          <li><strong>EOS framing:</strong> In EOS, the Integrator doesn't replace people — they organize and amplify the existing team. Frame your role as removing obstacles, not adding bureaucracy.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">🚩 Exit Timeline Pressure</div>
        <p><strong>The risk:</strong> 2–4 year exit + 2-year earnout means everything you build has to be "acquirer-ready." That creates pressure to prioritize optics (clean SOPs, pretty dashboards) over substance (deep operational improvement).</p>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Signal to watch:</strong> Does Kevin talk about the exit as "eventual" or "we're already talking to bankers"? The timeline urgency matters for your equity vesting and strategy prioritization.</li>
          <li><strong>Mitigant:</strong> The good news is that what acquirers want (systems, margins, scalable ops) is also what makes the company better. Align your work with both.</li>
          <li><strong>Question to ask:</strong> "How firm is the 2–4 year exit timeline? Have you engaged an investment bank yet?"</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">🚩 Compensation Risk: Heavy Equity, Light Cash</div>
        <p><strong>The risk:</strong> If the offer is equity-heavy with below-market base, you're betting on an exit that may or may not happen at the valuation you expect. PE-backed beauty companies can trade at 4–8x revenue, but market conditions change.</p>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Mitigant:</strong> Push for a base salary that's market-rate (or close) with equity as upside, not as compensation replacement. Your 5–8% equity target is aggressive but defensible — just make sure the base doesn't require the exit to work financially.</li>
          <li><strong>Key protection:</strong> Acceleration clause on equity if the company is sold before full vesting. Also: anti-dilution protection if they raise capital.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

## Risk Mitigation Playbook
<!-- id: risks-mitigate -->

<h3>Your Pre-Acceptance Checklist</h3>
      <p>Before saying yes, validate these items:</p>
      <table>
        <tr><th>#</th><th>Item</th><th>How to Validate</th><th>Status</th></tr>
        <tr><td>1</td><td>Decision authority clearly defined</td><td>Written V/I accountability chart before start</td><td>⬜</td></tr>
        <tr><td>2</td><td>Equity terms in writing</td><td>Vesting schedule, acceleration, anti-dilution, valuation basis</td><td>⬜</td></tr>
        <tr><td>3</td><td>Exit timeline and banker status</td><td>Ask Kevin directly; verify with fractional CFO if possible</td><td>⬜</td></tr>
        <tr><td>4</td><td>Meet key direct reports before accepting</td><td>At least brief conversations with Brandon, VP Growth, Head of PD</td><td>⬜</td></tr>
        <tr><td>5</td><td>Understand current financial health</td><td>Ask to see last 3 months P&L (even high-level) — verify profitability claim</td><td>⬜</td></tr>
        <tr><td>6</td><td>Kevin's actual work schedule & availability</td><td>1–2x/week in office — will he be available for your first 30 days?</td><td>⬜</td></tr>
      </table>

      <div class="tip"><strong>The meta-signal:</strong> How Kevin responds to these questions IS the data. If he's transparent and welcomes the diligence, that's a great sign. If he's evasive or dismissive, that tells you about the working relationship you'd have.</div>
