## Context: What Kevin's Email Tells Us
<!-- id: kq-context -->

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Kevin's exact words</div>
  <p>"I wanted something to reference to dive deeper into your experiences on the next call. Given your fractional positions, perhaps listing out a few brands you worked with more closely / drove most impact for? Or whatever you feel is helpful."</p>

<p>This tells us three things:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>He's going to drill into your fractional work.</strong> He wants specifics — not "I consulted for brands," but "here's what I did, here's the impact."</li>
  <li><strong>He's trying to assess whether fractional experience = real experience.</strong> There's an implicit question: "Did you actually do the work, or did you advise from the sidelines?"</li>
  <li><strong>"Whatever you feel is helpful"</strong> is an invitation to shape the narrative. He's giving you the pen. Use it.</li>
</ul>

<div class="tip"><strong>Strategy for the call:</strong> Kevin will likely go client by client through whatever you send him. For each one, expect: "What did you actually do there? What was the outcome? Why did you leave?" Prepare a tight 2-minute version and a 5-minute deep-dive version for each engagement.</div>

## Curveball / Harder Questions
<!-- id: kq-curveball -->

<p>These are the questions that don't have a clean answer. Prepare for them so you're not caught off guard.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">The Uncomfortable Questions</div>
  <table>
    <tr><th style="width:45%">Question</th><th>Guidance</th></tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>"Your biggest company was $20M. We're 5x that. Why should I bet on you?"</strong></td>
      <td>Don't get defensive. Own it and reframe: "You're right. But you didn't build iRESTORE by hiring someone from P&G who'd managed a $500M P&L. You built it yourself from zero. I think you understand better than most that the skills that build are different from the skills that maintain. I'm a builder. And based on what you've told me about where your operations are, you need a builder more than you need a maintainer right now."</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>"What if Brandon is resentful of you coming in above him?"</strong></td>
      <td>"That's a real concern and I'd address it head-on. First, I'd want to understand from you what your vision is for Brandon's role going forward. If he stays, my job is to make him more successful — clear priorities, better tools, less ambiguity. I'd invest time building that relationship early, one-on-one. If the dynamic doesn't work despite genuine effort, we'd handle that together. But I wouldn't come in assuming it's adversarial — I'd come in assuming there's someone with institutional knowledge I can learn from."</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>"Have you ever failed at something significant?"</strong></td>
      <td>Be honest. The B2TM debt story is a good one: "I took on too much inventory debt during a high-growth period. It worked in the short term but constrained our options later. It's the biggest financial mistake I've made, and it fundamentally changed how I think about cash flow. Now it's literally the first thing I build visibility into for any business I work with."</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>"What if I disagree with your priorities?"</strong></td>
      <td>"Then we have a conversation. I'd want to understand your reasoning, and I'd share mine. If we still disagree after hearing each other out, I think the right default is to go with the CEO's call — it's your company. But I'd want us to agree on how we evaluate whether it worked, so we're learning either way. The worst outcome isn't disagreeing — it's pretending to agree and then not executing with full commitment."</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>"Are you willing to relocate / be in La Mirada regularly?"</strong></td>
      <td>Be clear on this. Kevin commutes from Brentwood to La Mirada. If you're willing to be in the office regularly, say so. If you want flexibility, be upfront: "I'm a big believer in being present — especially in the first 6 months. I want to be in the warehouse, in the office, meeting the team face-to-face. I'd expect to be on-site multiple days a week, especially early on. Over time, as systems are in place and trust is built, I think we'd naturally find the right rhythm."</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>"What comp are you expecting?" / "Last time you said you'd think about numbers — where'd you land?"</strong></td>
      <td><strong>DO NOT name a number or range.</strong> Talk about structure and variables instead: "Yeah, I've been thinking about it a lot actually. But the more I dig in, the more the <em>structure</em> matters more than the headline number. You mentioned phantom stock last time — I've been looking at the tax math on phantom versus real equity and the spread is huge, like 15-plus points in California. And then the exit valuation question — what you'd sell for, what a home run looks like — that turns a percentage into an actual number. So I'd rather understand those variables before I put a stake in the ground. What I can tell you is I'm not going to be unreasonable — I want to be aligned with a big outcome, not optimize for cash." → <strong>See the full Comp Deflection Playbook in the Compensation &amp; Negotiation section for detailed scripts, follow-up pressure responses, and verbatim transcript from your first meeting.</strong></td>
    </tr>
