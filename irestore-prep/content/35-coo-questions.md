## COO Thinking: 10 High-Signal Questions
<!-- id: coo-q-intro -->

<p class="muted">These questions test judgment, not résumé. They're the ones that separate people who've executed at VP level from people who've actually operated as a COO. Each answer is calibrated to iRESTORE's specific context.</p>

---

## Q1 — What Breaks First?
<!-- id: coo-q1 -->

<h3>"We're growing 80% year over year. What breaks first, and how do you know before it happens?"</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: Systems thinking, leading vs. lagging indicators, operational foresight.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "At iRESTORE, the warehouse is probably the first thing that breaks — you've already seen it with Q4. But the more useful answer is: what are the <em>leading</em> indicators before it breaks? For warehouse throughput, it's pick/pack time per order trending up and fulfillment SLA variance widening. For supply chain, it's inventory coverage weeks tightening and emergency air freight percentage creeping up. For finance, it's the monthly close getting slower and manual reconciliation errors appearing. The metric I'd stand up on Day 1 is a weekly capacity utilization dashboard: warehouse utilization %, factory lead time vs. plan, and cash conversion cycle. Those three tell me what's about to break before it does — not after."
</div>

## Q2 — What NOT to Do
<!-- id: coo-q2 -->

<h3>"Walk me through how you decide what NOT to do this quarter."</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: Prioritization discipline, understanding that COO leverage comes from focus, not activity.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "I start with a capacity model — what can this team actually absorb without quality degrading? At iRESTORE right now, with 80% growth and three concurrent departures, the real constraint isn't ideas, it's execution bandwidth. Every initiative has to clear two filters: does it move toward the exit thesis — building a $500M asset with scalable revenue, operational defensibility, and clean systems — and can we actually execute it without something else breaking? I'd also use EOS Rocks to enforce this: 3 to 7 priorities per quarter, full stop. If something doesn't connect to a Rock, it goes on the Issues List. Kevin generates ideas faster than any team can execute. My job is making sure the team only gets what they can do well."
</div>

## Q3 — CEO's 40% Capacity Idea
<!-- id: coo-q3 -->

<h3>"The CEO brings you an exciting new channel or product idea that would consume 40% of operational capacity. How do you handle that conversation?"</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: CEO-COO dynamic, managing up, pressure-testing ideas without killing them.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "My job isn't to say no — it's to make sure Kevin has the full picture so he can make an informed call. First, I quantify the capacity impact in specifics: which teams get pulled, what gets delayed or dropped, and what the downstream effects are on revenue or customer experience. Then I bring it back as a trade-off: 'We can do this — here's what it displaces, here's the cost, here's the timeline.' Nine times out of ten, the idea either survives scrutiny and we resource it properly, or Kevin decides the trade-off isn't worth it once he sees it clearly. What I never do is just say 'we can't do that.' That's the Brandon answer — the one that already frustrated Kevin and is part of why this role exists."
</div>

## Q4 — Two Heads Who Can't Work Together
<!-- id: coo-q4 -->

<h3>"You inherit a team where two department heads are strong individually but can't work together. What do you do?"</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: Organizational judgment, willingness to make hard people decisions vs. managing around problems indefinitely.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "First thing I do is figure out what kind of conflict it is. Style conflicts — different communication preferences, pace, risk tolerance — can be structured around. Values conflicts — one owns their mistakes and the other deflects, one's transparent and the other isn't — are fundamentally different and usually can't be fixed. I'd have direct 1:1s with each, observe them in a team setting, and be explicit about expectations: here's how I need you two to operate, here's the timeline, here's what good looks like. Sixty days. If they can't get there, I make the call on which person is more critical to the mission and whether the other can be repositioned. The worst outcome is letting it fester — it signals to the entire team that dysfunction is tolerated, and that's a culture problem that's much harder to unwind."
</div>

## Q5 — What Does $500M Exit Mean Operationally?
<!-- id: coo-q5 -->

<h3>"We want to hit a $500M exit valuation. What does that mean operationally — what would an acquirer actually be buying?"</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: Financial and strategic literacy, understanding the COO's job is to build an asset, not just run a company.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "Acquirers buy three things: revenue quality, systems, and team. Revenue quality means predictable and scalable — DTC cohort retention, Amazon search dominance, and B2B channels that give an acquirer institutional distribution they don't already have. Systems means the company can run without Kevin or me in the room — documented SOPs, financials that close in five days, demand planning that doesn't live in someone's head. Team means Longchuan's R&D capability, Junior's growth machine, and an ops org that doesn't require heroics. Every messy process we clean up adds exit multiple. Every key-person dependency we eliminate reduces the acquirer's risk discount. At $90M with 80% growth, we're probably at a 3–4x revenue multiple. Get the operational risk down and the revenue quality up, and that multiple expands."
</div>

## Q6 — Revenue Up 80%, Margin Flat
<!-- id: coo-q6 -->

<h3>"Revenue is up 80% but margin is flat. What are your first three hypotheses and how do you test them?"</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: Financial acumen, diagnostic rigor, thinking in hypotheses not activities.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "Three hypotheses. One: channel mix shift — Amazon and Costco growing faster than DTC, dragging blended margin down. I'd test this by building a margin bridge by channel; if Amazon went from 40% to 55% of revenue, that alone could explain several points of compression. Two: input cost pressure — freight, tariffs, or COGS increases not offset by pricing. I'd look at gross margin by SKU over time, not just in aggregate. Three: fixed costs scaling proportionally instead of sublinearly. At 80% revenue growth, S&M and G&A should grow slower than revenue — if they're not, we have a leverage problem. Start with the P&L bridge: parse revenue by channel and SKU, gross margin by product, then OpEx line by line. The answer is almost always in one or two buckets."
</div>

## Q7 — Problem the CEO Won't Prioritize
<!-- id: coo-q7 -->

<h3>"You're six months in and you've identified a critical operational problem that the CEO doesn't see as a priority. What do you do?"</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: Influence without authority, building credibility, navigating conflict with the person who hired you.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "I don't accept 'not a priority' as final without one real conversation — but I also don't ambush or escalate prematurely. First, I translate the problem into terms Kevin cares about: dollars, customer impact, or exit multiple. 'Our warehouse SLA is degrading' is easy to deprioritize. 'We're processing 12% of orders late, which drives a 23% higher return rate on those orders — that's roughly $800K in preventable returns this quarter' is much harder to dismiss. Second, I bring a solution and a cost, not just a problem. Third, I time it right — don't surface an ops risk during a launch sprint. If after all that it's still deprioritized, I document my recommendation with the date and the data, and I revisit it in 60 days. Some things need to percolate. But I don't let critical risks sit unreported."
</div>

## Q8 — When Does Process Become a Liability?
<!-- id: coo-q8 -->

<h3>"At what point does process become a liability instead of an asset, and how do you know which side of that line you're on?"</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: Operational philosophy maturity, bureaucracy risk, contextual vs. dogmatic thinking about process.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "When it's optimizing for a risk profile you no longer have, or slowing decisions faster than it improves them. The signals: people are routing around the process because it's too slow, you have more process-managers than executors, and the default answer to a novel situation is 'let me check the process' instead of 'let me think about this.' At iRESTORE's stage — $90M, 80% growth, 80 people — you need enough structure to prevent things from falling through the cracks across a larger org, but not so much that you can't move on an opportunistic channel or launch. EOS gives you the right scaffolding: Rocks, L10 meetings, the Issues List. That's process that creates alignment without bureaucracy. I'd use EOS as the foundation and consciously resist layering on top of it."
</div>

## Q9 — Supply Chain Crisis: First 72 Hours
<!-- id: coo-q9 -->

<h3>"A major supply chain disruption hits during your highest-demand season. Walk me through your first 72 hours."</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: Crisis structure, judgment under pressure, communication vs. analysis paralysis.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "First hour: establish facts — specific SKU, specific carrier, specific region. You can't manage a crisis with assumptions. Within 4 hours: triage by SLA priority. Costco's 1-day SLA is first, FBA is second, DTC is third — preserve the relationships with the highest penalty for failure. By end of day 1: proactive customer communication. The silence is always worse than the news. Set expectations, be honest, give a timeline. By hour 48: alternative routing active — spot freight, backup carrier, regional reallocation if available. Hour 72: post-mortem started, not after recovery. Document the failure mode while it's live, not from memory months later. Throughout: daily brief to Kevin — three things only: current damage, what we're doing, what I need from you. He doesn't want to manage the crisis. He wants to know it's being managed."
</div>

## Q10 — When to Tell the CEO to Slow Down
<!-- id: coo-q10 -->

<h3>"What would make you tell a CEO that the company should slow down growth?"</h3>
<p class="muted"><em>Probes: Intellectual courage, conviction to challenge growth-at-all-costs when the fundamentals warrant it.</em></p>
<div class="answer-block">
  <strong>Model Answer:</strong> "When growth is destroying more value than it's creating. Three specific signals: cash burning faster than receivables can cover — negative working capital that can't be financed; customer acquisition outrunning fulfillment quality — returns and churn accelerating because you're spending to acquire customers you can't serve well, which destroys brand equity faster than revenue builds it; and the team making more errors per dollar of revenue as a function of overload — compliance issues, operational mistakes, things that create future liability. The conversation with Kevin would be: 'Here's the pace we're on, here's the constraint that breaks before we get there, here's what it costs if it breaks, and here's what we'd need to invest to make it safe — what do you want to do?' That's not telling him to slow down. That's giving him the information to make the call. My job is to make the trade-offs visible, not to make the decision for him."
</div>
