## Offer Response Playbook
<!-- id: offer-playbook -->

<h3>Rule #1: Never React on the Call</h3>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">The Only Response That Matters</div>
  <p><strong>No matter what the number is — even if it's amazing — take time to digest the specifics.</strong></p>
  <p>Every response below ends with some version of: <em>"I'm really excited about this — let me take a couple days to go through the details and I'll come back to you."</em></p>
  <p><strong>Show enthusiasm for the role. Take time on the numbers.</strong> Kevin is a founder who cares about people giving a shit. Full poker face = "this is just a transaction." You want him to know you're all in on the opportunity — you're just being thoughtful about the details. Both things can be true.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">If Kevin Pushes for a Live Reaction</div>
  <ul>
    <li>"I just want to give you a thoughtful response rather than a knee-jerk one. I owe you that."</li>
    <li>"I'd rather come back with a real answer than say something half-baked right now."</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<h3>Equity Response Scripts</h3>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">0.5% - 1%: The "That's Quite Low" Response</div>
  <p>"Okay, I appreciate you sharing that. I'll be honest, that's quite a bit lower than where I thought we'd land, but I want to take the time to model it out properly before I react. Can I come back to you in a day or two?"</p>
  <p><strong>Then:</strong> Come back with the spreadsheet. Show him the after-tax math. If he's anchored here, you probably can't negotiate up enough to make it work. This may be a signal that he doesn't value the role at the level you need.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">1.5% - 2%: The "I Need to Model This" Response</div>
  <p>"Got it, I appreciate the transparency. I have some thoughts but I want to sit with the numbers and run through the scenarios before I respond. Give me a couple days?"</p>
  <p><strong>Then:</strong> Come back with: "Kevin, I've modeled this out carefully. At 2% phantom, after CA taxes, we're talking about a net number that honestly doesn't clear the bar for me given what I'd be walking away from. I'm not negotiating for sport — I genuinely want to do this. But I need the equity at a level where the math works. For me that's in the [X]% range, and here's why..." Pull up spreadsheet.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">2.5% - 3%: The "Interesting Range" Response</div>
  <p>"I appreciate that. Definitely in a more interesting range — let me take a day to digest and model it out, and I'll come back to you with my thoughts."</p>
  <p><strong>Then:</strong> Propose the <strong>tiered structure</strong> — base grant at his number + performance acceleration for an additional 1-2% tied to specific milestones (EBITDA improvement, exit readiness benchmarks). This lets Kevin feel comfortable on the starting number while giving you a path to 4-5%.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">3.5% - 4.5%: The "Meaningful" Response</div>
  <p>"That's meaningful, I appreciate it. I want to run through the math on my end and think about how it looks across the different scenarios we talked about. Let me come back to you shortly."</p>
  <p><strong>Then:</strong> Negotiate structure, not percentage. Push for: acceleration on change of control, protection in partial-sale scenarios, and clarity on payout triggers. This is also where you push hardest on phantom vs. real equity structure — at this percentage, the tax delta is worth millions.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">5%+: The "Don't Blow It" Response</div>
  <p>"That's a strong offer and I really appreciate it. Let me take a day to look at the full picture and I'll come back to you."</p>
  <p><strong>Then:</strong> Do NOT negotiate the percentage. Focus entirely on structural protections: change of control triggers, what happens in partial sale, vesting acceleration, phantom vs. real equity if possible. Don't give him any reason to second-guess the number. Close the deal.</p>
</div>

<h3>The Spreadsheet Move</h3>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">When to Use It</div>
  <p>The after-tax scenario spreadsheet (<code>phantom_equity_scenarios.xlsx</code>) is your most powerful tool if the equity offer is 1-3%. It reframes the conversation from Kevin's gross mental math to your net reality.</p>
  <p><strong>How to deploy it:</strong></p>
  <ol>
    <li>Pull it up on a call (share screen) or bring it to an in-person meeting</li>
    <li>Use the <strong>clean version only</strong> — no color coding, no "hell yes" labels</li>
    <li>Say: <em>"I put together a simple model so we're looking at the same math."</em></li>
    <li>Ask Kevin: <em>"Where do you think the exit lands?"</em> — let HIM pick the column</li>
    <li>Then you're both staring at the same row and the number either works or it doesn't</li>
  </ol>
  <p><strong>Why this works:</strong> Kevin is almost certainly thinking in gross, pre-tax terms. When he sees that his "generous" 2% = $4.7M net to you at a $500M exit, the gap becomes obvious without you having to argue for it.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Note on the 52.65% Tax Rate</div>
  <p>This is the combined CA ordinary income rate for phantom stock payouts:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Federal: ~37% top marginal rate</li>
    <li>CA state: ~13.3%</li>
    <li>Additional Medicare: ~2.35%</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Kevin may push back on this number or not fully understand it. Be prepared to walk through it. The tax rate is not negotiable — it's just math. But it IS a reason to discuss alternative structures (real equity with LTCG treatment at ~37.1%).</p>
</div>

<h3>The Tiered Equity Proposal</h3>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Your Ace Card: Performance-Based Escalation</div>
  <p>If Kevin offers 3% and you want 5%, don't just counter with a higher number. Propose a structure that gives Kevin downside protection while giving you upside:</p>
  <table style="width:100%; border-collapse:collapse; margin-top:8px;">
    <thead>
      <tr style="background:#1a1a3e; color:white;">
        <th style="padding:10px;">Component</th>
        <th style="padding:10px;">Amount</th>
        <th style="padding:10px;">Condition</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr><td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Base grant</strong></td><td style="padding:10px;">3% (Kevin's number)</td><td style="padding:10px;">Standard vesting (4yr / 1yr cliff)</td></tr>
      <tr><td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Performance tier 1</strong></td><td style="padding:10px;">+1%</td><td style="padding:10px;">Hit specific EBITDA or margin improvement targets within 18 months</td></tr>
      <tr><td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Performance tier 2</strong></td><td style="padding:10px;">+1%</td><td style="padding:10px;">Business achieves exit-readiness benchmarks / revenue milestone</td></tr>
      <tr><td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Exit bonus</strong></td><td style="padding:10px;">+0.5-1%</td><td style="padding:10px;">Exit exceeds a specific threshold (e.g., $500M+)</td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
  <p style="margin-top:12px;"><strong>Why this works for Kevin:</strong> He's not giving away 5-6% on day one to an unproven COO. He's giving 3% with a path to more if you deliver.</p>
  <p><strong>Why this works for you:</strong> Path to 5-6% that you control through your own performance. And it demonstrates exactly the kind of structured, incentive-aligned thinking a COO should bring.</p>
</div>

<h3>Traps to Avoid</h3>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Trap: "The other candidates are asking for X%"</div>
  <p>Whether true or not, this is designed to compress your ask.</p>
  <p><strong>Counter:</strong> "I'd expect them to — they're pricing it differently because they're bringing a different profile. I'm not benchmarking against other candidates, I'm benchmarking against what it takes for me to go all in."</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Trap: High cash to offset low equity</div>
  <p>Kevin offers $450K base + 2% phantom. Feels generous. The math: even at $500M exit, you net $4.7M on equity. The extra $50-100K/year in salary doesn't come close to making up for 2-3 missing equity points.</p>
  <p><strong>Counter:</strong> "I really appreciate the cash number — that's strong. But as I mentioned, equity is what drives this decision. I'd actually prefer to moderate cash if it means a more meaningful equity position."</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Trap: "That's X million dollars at exit"</div>
  <p>Kevin quotes the gross number: "2% of a $500M exit is $10 million — that's life-changing money." He's not wrong on the gross. But after 52.65% phantom tax, it's $4.7M.</p>
  <p><strong>Counter:</strong> "I hear you, and I appreciate the intent. But let me show you what that looks like after CA taxes on phantom stock..." — then pull up the spreadsheet.</p>
</div>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Trap: Short deadline to respond</div>
  <p>Kevin says "I need an answer by Friday." This creates pressure to accept without fully modeling or negotiating.</p>
  <p><strong>Counter:</strong> "I'm very excited about this and I want to get to yes. I just want to make sure I'm being thoughtful about it so there are no surprises for either of us. Can we do [X day]?" A serious offer can wait 3-5 business days.</p>
</div>

<h3>Structural Points to Negotiate</h3>

<p>Regardless of what percentage you land on, these are the structural protections to push for in the phantom stock agreement:</p>

<table style="width:100%; border-collapse:collapse; margin-top:12px;">
  <thead>
    <tr style="background:#1a1a3e; color:white;">
      <th style="padding:10px; text-align:left;">Issue</th>
      <th style="padding:10px; text-align:left;">What You Want</th>
      <th style="padding:10px; text-align:left;">Why It Matters</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Change of control trigger</strong></td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Full acceleration + payout on any change of control event (sale, merger, acquisition)</td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Without this, a buyer could restructure and your phantom stock could become worthless</td>
    </tr>
    <tr style="background:#f8f9fa;">
      <td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Partial sale / PE investment</strong></td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Pro-rata payout if Kevin sells a portion of the company or takes PE investment</td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Kevin could take $20-30M off the table via recap or partial sale with zero trigger on your phantom</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Definition of "sale"</strong></td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Broad definition: sale of assets, sale of equity, merger, IPO, recapitalization above a threshold</td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Narrow definitions (e.g., "100% sale of company only") create loopholes</td>
    </tr>
    <tr style="background:#f8f9fa;">
      <td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Vesting acceleration</strong></td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Single-trigger acceleration on change of control (all unvested units vest immediately)</td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">If the company sells before your vest completes, you don't want to lose unvested equity</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Termination without cause</strong></td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Vested phantom stock survives termination and pays out at next liquidity event</td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Without this, getting fired = losing everything even if vested</td>
    </tr>
    <tr style="background:#f8f9fa;">
      <td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Sunset / put option</strong></td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Right to force redemption after 5-7 years if no liquidity event has occurred</td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">If Kevin decides not to sell, your phantom could sit forever with no payout mechanism</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Good Reason definition</strong></td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Material role reduction, salary cut, forced relocation, or reporting structure change = can resign and retain vested phantom</td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Protects you if a post-acquisition buyer demotes or sidelines you to avoid payouts</td>
    </tr>
    <tr style="background:#f8f9fa;">
      <td style="padding:10px;"><strong>Involvement in exit decisions</strong></td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">Seat at the table for strategic discussions about timing and terms of any exit</td>
      <td style="padding:10px;">As a meaningful equity holder, you should not learn about an exit when everyone else does</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<div class="card" style="margin-top:16px;">
  <div class="card-header">Prioritize: Pick 3</div>
  <p>Per M&amp;A best practices: don't negotiate 25 points when you care about 3. The structural points above are listed in rough priority order. <strong>Change of control trigger, termination protection, and broad definition of "sale"</strong> are the three that matter most. Fight for those, be flexible on the rest.</p>
</div>
