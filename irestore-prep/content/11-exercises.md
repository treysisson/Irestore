## Scenario: Demand Planning (RAPID)
<!-- id: ex-demand -->

<h3>Scenario A: Demand Forecasting & Inventory Planning</h3>
      <p>You're heading into Q4 with a new Costco.com launch, strong Amazon trends, and a DTC promo calendar. How do you build the demand plan?</p>

      <p><span class="label">Framework: RAPID</span> — an easy-to-remember framework for demand planning decisions. Also reference S&OP (Sales & Operations Planning) as the industry-standard monthly cadence.</p>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">RAPID Framework</div>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>R — Review Signals:</strong> Aggregate demand signals from every channel. Amazon BSR trends + PPC spend plans, DTC promo calendar, Costco PO commitments, wholesale pipeline. Weight each by historical accuracy.</li>
          <li><strong>A — Align Supply:</strong> Map demand against manufacturing lead times (Shenzhen: ~60–90 days production + 30–35 days ocean freight). Identify the "commit point" — the last date to place a PO and have it arrive in time. This is the critical decision date.</li>
          <li><strong>P — Position Inventory:</strong> Decide where to stage inventory. FBA has 90-day storage limits. 3PL gives flexibility but adds a shipment leg. Pre-position Costco allocation separately (their SLAs are non-negotiable).</li>
          <li><strong>I — Identify Scenarios:</strong> Build three scenarios (base / upside / downside). Set safety stock to cover the gap between base and upside for top 5 SKUs. This is what prevents the Q4 crisis from happening again.</li>
          <li><strong>D — Decide Together (Executive S&OP):</strong> Monthly review with Kevin where demand, supply, and finance align on one number. This is the meeting that prevents surprises. In EOS terms, this maps to the quarterly Rock-setting cadence.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="tip"><strong>Why RAPID works in an interview:</strong> It's memorable, it shows structured thinking, and each letter maps to a real operational step. S&OP is the textbook term; RAPID is how you explain it in 60 seconds.</div>

      <div class="answer-block"><strong>Sample Answer:</strong> "The core issue is that demand planning can't be a Q3 exercise for Q4 — it has to be continuous. I use a framework I call RAPID: Review demand signals across channels, Align supply against lead times, Position inventory by channel, Identify upside/downside scenarios with safety stock, and Decide together in a monthly S&OP meeting with the leadership team. For iRESTORE, the critical piece is the 'commit point' — with 60–90 day production lead times in Shenzhen plus 30 days of ocean freight, you need to be making Q4 decisions in early Q2. That's the cadence that prevents surprises."</div>

## Scenario: Warehouse Crisis (Q4)
<!-- id: ex-fulfillment -->

<h3>What Actually Happened</h3>

<div class="warning"><strong>This is Kevin's defining operational frustration.</strong> It's the story he told to explain why he's hiring a COO. Every detail of your answer will be measured against this real experience.</div>

<p>Costco ran a promotion that 10x'd their forecast volume. Because Costco has a <strong>1-day SLA</strong> (non-negotiable — miss it and you risk losing the account), the La Mirada warehouse had to prioritize Costco fulfillment. All DTC orders backed up for ~2 weeks. Customer complaints spiked. Amazon seller metrics started degrading. The warehouse was at capacity, the staffing agency couldn't provide more people, and Brandon's response was <em>"we can't do anything."</em></p>

<p><strong>The real problem wasn't inventory</strong> — they had product. It was a <strong>throughput crisis</strong> at their own facility: not enough labor, not enough packing stations, no contingency plan, and no one had modeled what would happen if two channels peaked simultaneously. The deeper failure: nobody was doing capacity planning or scenario modeling for peak. Brandon was managing reactively, week to week.</p>

<h3>Phase 1: How to Prevent It (Q2–Q3 Planning)</h3>

<p>This is the part that demonstrates you're a different kind of operator than Brandon. You don't wait for Q4 to think about Q4.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Step 1: Build the Peak Scenario Model (April–May)</div>
  <p>Start with last year's Q4 data. Then layer in growth assumptions:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Base case:</strong> 40% over last Q4 (consistent with 80% YoY growth, seasonally weighted)</li>
    <li><strong>Upside case:</strong> 80% over last Q4 (if Costco promotion + Amazon holiday + DTC Black Friday all peak simultaneously)</li>
    <li><strong>Stress case:</strong> Single channel does 5–10x forecast for 1–2 weeks (exactly what happened)</li>
  </ul>
  <p>For each scenario, calculate: daily orders by channel, required pick/pack labor hours, packing station utilization, and carrier pickup capacity. The output is a simple table: <strong>"On our worst day, we need X people, Y stations, and Z carrier pickups. Today we can do A, B, C. The gap is D, E, F."</strong></p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Step 2: Pre-Position Surge Labor (June–July)</div>
  <p><strong>The principle:</strong> Never rely on one source for anything. Two agencies, two platforms, two plans.</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Traditional temp agencies (contract by July):</strong>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Randstad</strong> or <strong>Robert Half</strong> — national players with LA warehouse divisions. Reliable but slower (48–72 hr lead time). Best for planned surge weeks.</li>
        <li><strong>A local agency</strong> near La Mirada (e.g., Great Hire Staffing in the Cerritos/La Mirada corridor). Smaller but faster — they know the local labor pool. Often can get bodies in 24 hours.</li>
        <li>Pre-negotiate rates: $18–22/hr fully loaded. Have a standing agreement: "We'll need 15–20 people for 6–8 weeks starting mid-October. Here's the job description. Pre-screen them now."</li>
      </ul>
    </li>
    <li><strong>On-demand labor platforms (set up accounts by August):</strong>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Instawork</strong> — 7M+ workers, strong in LA warehouse shifts. Post shifts and get workers within hours. No long-term commitment. Best for "we need 10 people tomorrow" situations.</li>
        <li><strong>Veryable</strong> — purpose-built for warehouse/manufacturing surge. Workers bid on shifts. Next-day labor. Good for ongoing flex capacity.</li>
        <li><strong>Wonolo</strong> — similar model, specializes in warehousing. Post a shift at 6 PM, have workers at 6 AM.</li>
        <li><strong>Why platforms + agencies:</strong> Agencies are better for multi-week commitments (consistency). Platforms are better for day-of surge (speed). Use both.</li>
      </ul>
    </li>
    <li><strong>Internal "friends and family" list:</strong> Ask every warehouse employee to identify 2–3 people they'd vouch for who could work a temp shift. Pre-collect names and phone numbers. Offer a $100 referral bonus per person who works a full week. This is the fastest, cheapest labor source and Kevin explicitly wished Brandon had done this.</li>
    <li><strong>College/community labor:</strong> La Mirada is near Biola University, Cerritos College, and Cal State Fullerton. Post to campus job boards in September: "$20/hr, flexible shifts, Oct–Dec." College students are reliable for evening/weekend shifts.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Step 2b: Pre-Position Overflow Fulfillment (June–July)</div>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Local 3PL on retainer:</strong>

<h3>Phase 2: When Crisis Hits Anyway (Real-Time Triage)</h3>

<p>Even with planning, something will go wrong. Here's the decision tree:</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Hour 1–4: Assess and Quantify</div>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Quantify the backlog in hours, not orders.</strong> "We're 2,000 orders behind" means nothing. "At current throughput, we're 36 hours behind and falling further" — that's actionable.</li>
    <li><strong>Identify the specific bottleneck:</strong> Is it pick labor? Pack stations? Carrier pickup windows? Inventory in wrong zone? Each has a different solution.</li>
    <li><strong>Calculate the bleed rate:</strong> How many more orders are coming in per hour vs. how many we're shipping? If inbound > outbound, the backlog grows. You need to know the crossover point.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Hour 4–24: Activate Everything (Simultaneously)</div>
  <p>Don't sequence these — launch all at once. You need bodies, space, and throughput as fast as possible.</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Call both temp agencies:</strong> "I need 15 people tomorrow morning, 15 more the day after. Warehouse general labor, we'll train on site. Rate is $20/hr. This is the surge we discussed in July."</li>
    <li><strong>Post shifts on Instawork + Veryable:</strong> "Warehouse pick/pack, $22/hr, 6 AM – 2 PM, La Mirada. No experience needed." Higher rate than agencies because you need speed — platform workers can show up same-day or next-day.</li>
    <li><strong>Text the friends-and-family list:</strong> "We need extra hands this week. $20/hr, flexible hours, start tomorrow. Reply YES if interested." Brandon's team of 25 people, each with 2–3 contacts = 50–75 potential workers. Even a 20% response rate gives you 10–15 people within 24 hours.</li>
    <li><strong>Activate 3PL overflow:</strong> Call OLIMP/Ideal/whoever you've contracted. "We're activating the overflow agreement. Sending a truck with 500 units of our top 5 SKUs tomorrow morning, plus packing materials and labels. Your team picks, packs, and ships DTC orders from the file we'll send hourly via CSV."</li>
    <li><strong>Stand up the second shift:</strong> Your pre-identified shift leads stay for 4 PM – midnight with a mix of experienced staff (voluntary OT) and platform workers. First night is 50% training, 50% productive. Second night is 80% productive.</li>
    <li><strong>Pull office staff:</strong> Not to pick/pack (they'll be slow), but to: sort inbound, stage outbound pallets, apply shipping labels, run QC checks, answer CS tickets about delays. Everyone can contribute something. This also builds culture — "we're all in this together."</li>

## Live Exercise: P&L Analysis
<!-- id: ex-pnl-live -->

<div class="warning"><strong>HIGH PROBABILITY.</strong> This is the most likely live exercise. Kevin hands you a real (or simplified) P&L and asks what you see.</div>

<h3>How It Will Probably Work</h3>
<p>Kevin prints or screens a P&L — maybe the full thing, maybe just a summary — and says something like: "Here's our financials. Walk me through what jumps out to you." He's testing whether you can read a P&L fluently, think like an operator (not an accountant), and identify the right levers without being told.</p>

<h3>Your Framework (in this order)</h3>
<ol>
  <li><strong>Orient out loud:</strong> "Let me start with the shape of the business — revenue, gross margin, then work down." This shows structured thinking and buys you 10 seconds to scan.</li>
  <li><strong>Anchor to gross margin first:</strong> Is it 60%+? That means the product economics work. Below 55%? That's a product/sourcing problem before anything else.</li>
  <li><strong>Find where margin gets absorbed:</strong> Walk from gross profit to EBITDA. Name each cost bucket as a percentage of revenue. Look for anything that feels out of benchmark.</li>
  <li><strong>Identify the 2–3 biggest levers:</strong> Don't list 10 things. Pick the 2–3 line items where small improvements drive the biggest dollar impact.</li>
  <li><strong>Ask a smart follow-up:</strong> "Can I see this broken out by channel?" or "How much of this marketing line is acquisition vs. retention?" This shows you know what to dig into next.</li>
</ol>

<h3>What to Scan on Any P&L (in 30 Seconds)</h3>
<p>Contribution margin isn't a line item you'll see on a P&L — it's something you have to derive. Here's exactly which <em>actual</em> line items to scan and what math to do in your head:</p>

<table>
  <tr><th>Line Item on P&L</th><th>What It Tells You</th><th>Red Flag Threshold</th></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Gross Revenue</strong> vs. <strong>Net Revenue</strong></td><td>The gap = returns + allowances. If the gap is &gt;12%, returns are a problem.</td><td>Gap &gt; 12% of gross</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>COGS</strong> (as % of net rev)</td><td>Product economics. DTC beauty should be 28–38%. Above 40% = sourcing or tariff issue.</td><td>&gt; 40% of net rev</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Amazon Referral Fees</strong></td><td>~8–15% of Amazon GMV. If higher, check category classification.</td><td>&gt; 15% of Amazon rev</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>FBA / Fulfillment Fees</strong></td><td>Amazon FBA = 12–18% of Amazon rev. 3PL/in-house for DTC should be 8–12%.</td><td>Amazon &gt; 18%; DTC &gt; 12%</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Advertising / PPC</strong></td><td>Amazon ACoS + Meta/Google spend. The biggest variable cost after COGS.</td><td>&gt; 20% of net rev blended</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Wholesale Discounts</strong></td><td>Costco/retail margin concessions. Usually 30–40% off retail.</td><td>&gt; 40% discount</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Return Processing / Freight</strong></td><td>The hidden cost of returns — not just the refund, but the reverse logistics.</td><td>&gt; 3% of net rev</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>G&amp;A / OpEx</strong></td><td>Headcount + rent + software + overhead. Should be 12–18% at this scale.</td><td>&gt; 20% of net rev</td></tr>
</table>

<div class="tip"><strong>Quick mental math:</strong> Start at Gross Profit (net rev minus COGS). Then subtract each cost bucket you see between GP and EBITDA. Whatever's left after marketing + fulfillment + channel fees = the channel's "contribution" to covering overhead and profit. You don't need a line labeled "contribution margin" — you're just doing GP minus variable costs in your head.</div>

<table>
  <tr><th>Min</th><th>Segment</th><th>What You Do</th></tr>
  <tr><td>0–5</td><td><strong>Segue</strong></td><td>Each person shares one personal + one professional good thing from the week. Keeps it human. Don't skip this — it's how EOS builds trust.</td></tr>

  <tr><td>10–15</td><td><strong>Rock Review</strong></td><td>Each person reports their quarterly Rocks as "on track" or "off track." Again — only discuss off-track. This is not a status meeting.</td></tr>

<h3>If They Ask You to Facilitate a Mock IDS</h3>
<p>Kevin or someone presents a real issue. Here's your playbook:</p>
<ol>
  <li><strong>"Let me make sure I understand the issue."</strong> Restate it in one sentence. Often the stated issue isn't the real issue. Probe: "Is that the symptom, or is there something underneath that?"</li>
  <li><strong>Timebox discussion:</strong> "Let's give this 7 minutes. If we can't solve it here, we'll assign it as a to-do for offline work."</li>
  <li><strong>Drive to a decision:</strong> "I'm hearing two options — [A] and [B]. Does anyone see a third option, or should we decide between these two?"</li>
  <li><strong>Assign the to-do:</strong> "Who owns this, and when will it be done?" Not "the team" — one person, one date.</li>
</ol>

<div class="answer-block"><strong>If asked about EOS philosophy:</strong> "EOS works because it creates clarity — everyone knows the vision, everyone knows their number, and every issue gets solved in the same disciplined way. ## COO Leadership Traits: What to Cultivate
<!-- id: ex-coo-traits -->

<p>Being a people magnet is one trait. Here are the others that separate COOs who last a decade from ones who flame out in 18 months — and how to signal each one in the interview <em>and</em> build it for the long run.</p>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">1. Pattern Recognition Across Silos</div>
  <p><strong>What it is:</strong> Seeing connections across departments that nobody else sees. Marketing's CAC is climbing? You notice it correlates with warehouse shipping delays from two weeks ago — late deliveries tanked repeat purchase rates. Finance flagged rising COGS? You trace it to a product mix shift that came from a growth team decision.</p>
  <p><strong>How to build it:</strong> For every problem you encounter, force yourself to ask: "What's upstream and downstream of this?" Build the habit of reading across functions — not just your lane. The COO who only understands ops is a VP of Ops. The COO who understands how ops connects to finance, marketing, product, and customer experience is irreplaceable.</p>
  <p><strong>Signal in the interview:</strong> When Kevin presents any scenario, connect it to at least two other functions. "If warehouse throughput drops, that's not just a fulfillment problem — it hits DTC NPS, Amazon seller metrics, and potentially our Costco fill rate. I'd want to trace this upstream to see if it's a demand planning miss or a labor issue."</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">2. Radical Intellectual Honesty</div>
  <p><strong>What it is:</strong> Telling the CEO what they need to hear, not what they want to hear — and always pairing the hard truth with a path forward. Not just identifying problems (that's easy and annoying), but pairing every uncomfortable observation with a plan.</p>
  <p><strong>How to build it:</strong> Practice the formula: <strong>honest observation + context + recommendation</strong>. Never deliver a diagnosis without a prescription. "Our Amazon margins are thinner than they look because FBA fees are eating 13% — but here's a 90-day plan to recover 2–3 points through commingling optimization and FBM testing on slow movers."</p>
  <p><strong>Signal in the interview:</strong> Push back on something. Respectfully disagree with an assumption in a scenario. Kevin isn't hiring a yes-person — he's been the only decision-maker for years. He needs someone who'll say "I see it differently, and here's why."</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">3. The Translator (Visionary ↔ Execution)</div>
  <p><strong>What it is:</strong> Taking the CEO's big ideas — "let's launch in Japan" — and turning them into sequenced, resourced, realistic plans <em>without killing the energy behind the idea</em>. And taking the team's ground-level reality — "we can't ship 40K units in December with this layout" — and bringing it to Kevin in a way that's solution-oriented, not defeatist.</p>
  <p><strong>How to build it:</strong> When someone gives you a big vision, your first response should never be "that won't work." It should be: "Here's what it would take." When someone gives you a ground-level complaint, reframe it: "What would need to be true for this to work?" You become fluent in both languages — vision and execution — by practicing the translation in real time.</p>
  <p><strong>Signal in the interview:</strong> When Kevin talks about international expansion or new product categories, respond with <em>enthusiasm + structure</em>: "I love that. Here's how I'd sequence it — Phase 1 is X, Phase 2 is Y, and the decision point between them is Z."</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">4. Comfortable Being the Second Name</div>
  <p><strong>What it is:</strong> The COO does 70% of the work and gets 10% of the credit. Kevin will be the face of iRESTORE on Shark Tank, at trade shows, in press. Your wins show up as his wins. The COOs who thrive in this genuinely get their satisfaction from watching the machine run well — not from external recognition.</p>
  <p><strong>How to build it:</strong> Redefine what success looks like for you. The scoreboard isn't your LinkedIn profile — it's: Did the team hit their numbers? Is Kevin spending time on vision instead of putting out fires? Are people growing? Is the company more valuable than when you started? If you keep score on credit, it'll poison the relationship.</p>
  <p><strong>Signal in the interview:</strong> Talk about past wins in terms of team outcomes, not personal glory. "We took the warehouse from 92% to 99.4% accuracy" not "I implemented a new WMS." Use "we" more than "I."</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">5. Decisive Under Ambiguity</div>
  <p><strong>What it is:</strong> You'll never have enough data. The warehouse is at 85% capacity, holiday season is coming, Costco PO could be 20K or 50K units, and you need to decide on a 3PL contract this week. The best COOs make a call, document their reasoning, build in a fallback, and move.</p>
  <p><strong>How to build it:</strong> Practice the framework: <strong>"Here's what I'd do, here's what changes if I'm wrong, and here's when we'll know."</strong> Stop waiting for perfect information. Get comfortable with 70% confidence decisions. The cost of waiting is almost always higher than the cost of being wrong — because wrong decisions can be corrected, but lost time can't be recovered. This is especially true at $90M growing 80%: you're making decisions with last quarter's data about next quarter's reality.</p>
  <p><strong>Signal in the interview:</strong> When given a scenario with incomplete information, don't ask for more data. Make a call and explain your reasoning: "With what I know, I'd do X. The risk is Y. My fallback is Z. I'd revisit in two weeks when we have [specific data]."</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">6. Process Architect, Not Process Bureaucrat</div>
  <p><strong>What it is:</strong> Building systems and processes — EOS, SOPs, dashboards, cadences — but knowing when to break them. A company growing 80% YoY can't run like a Fortune 500. The best COOs build processes that are tight enough to create consistency but loose enough to absorb chaos.</p>
  <p><strong>How to build it:</strong> For every process you create, ask: "Does this serve the work, or does the work now serve this process?" Know which processes are sacred (financial controls, inventory accuracy, safety) and which are guidelines (meeting formats, approval workflows). Kill bureaucracy before it kills velocity. The test: if removing a process wouldn't cause a measurable problem within 30 days, it probably shouldn't exist.</p>
  <p><strong>Signal in the interview:</strong> Show you know the difference. "I'd implement a demand planning cadence that's locked — non-negotiable, every week. But I'd keep the approval process for marketing spend flexible, because at your growth rate, a three-day approval cycle costs you more than a few bad $5K bets."</p>
</div>

<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">7. Emotional Regulation as a Superpower</div>
  <p><strong>What it is:</strong> When the container ship is stuck, the Costco buyer is furious, and three people quit the same Tuesday — the COO is the person who can't lose composure. Not in a "stuff your feelings" way, but in a "I process fast and redirect energy toward solutions" way. The team takes their emotional cues from you.</p>
  <p><strong>How to build it:</strong> This is trainable. It's reps. The more crises you navigate, the wider your window of tolerance gets. Build a personal protocol: when something goes sideways, your first move is always to gather facts before reacting. Take 30 seconds. Ask: "What do we actually know? What's the real blast radius? What's the first decision that needs to happen?" The calm is contagious — and so is panic.</p>
  <p><strong>Signal in the interview:</strong> Tell a story about a crisis where you stayed calm. Not just "I handled it" — describe the moment where everyone was losing it and what you specifically did to redirect energy. Kevin needs to know you won't add to his stress during the hard weeks.</p>
</div>

<div class="answer-block"><strong>The thread connecting all seven:</strong> The COO's job is to make everyone around them better at their jobs. Not to be the smartest person in the room, not to have all the answers, but to create the conditions where good people do great work and the CEO can focus on what only the CEO can do. For the interview, you don't need to claim all seven — demonstrating three or four through stories and through how you show up is enough. The people magnet piece, radical intellectual honesty, and decisiveness under ambiguity are probably your highest-leverage signals for Kevin's specific situation.</div>
