## The Visionary / Integrator Dynamic
<!-- id: eos-vi -->

<h3>Kevin = Visionary. You = Integrator.</h3>
      <p>In EOS, the Visionary and Integrator are the two seats at the top of the accountability chart. They have fundamentally different roles:</p>

      <table>
        <tr><th>Visionary (Kevin)</th><th>Integrator (You)</th></tr>
        <tr><td>Big ideas, product vision, relationships</td><td>Execution, systems, accountability</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Starts 10 things</td><td>Finishes 3 things well</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Inspires the team</td><td>Manages the team</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Works ON the business</td><td>Works IN the business</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Sees opportunities</td><td>Sees obstacles and solves them</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Culture / brand / innovation</td><td>Profit / people / process</td></tr>
      </table>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">The Same Page Meeting (Critical First Step)</div>
        <p>Before Day 1 (or during Week 1), you and Kevin need a <strong>Same Page Meeting</strong>. This is an EOS concept where the Visionary and Integrator explicitly align on:</p>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Roles:</strong> What does Kevin own? What do you own? Where's the overlap?</li>
          <li><strong>Communication:</strong> How often do you sync? What decisions require Kevin's input? What can you decide alone?</li>
          <li><strong>Conflict resolution:</strong> When you disagree (you will), how do you resolve it? EOS says: the Integrator makes the call on execution; the Visionary makes the call on vision.</li>
          <li><strong>People decisions:</strong> Hiring, firing, promotions — who has final say? (EOS says the Integrator owns this.)</li>
          <li><strong>The cardinal rule:</strong> The Visionary never goes around the Integrator to direct reports. Everything flows through the Integrator. This is the hardest rule for founders to follow.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="tip"><strong>Interview power move:</strong> Bring up the Same Page Meeting in the interview. "One of the first things I'd want to do is have a formal Same Page Meeting — it's the EOS framework for aligning the Visionary and Integrator. I want to make sure we're crystal clear on decision rights from Day 1." This shows you know EOS at a practitioner level, not just conceptually.</div>

## EOS Implementation Roadmap
<!-- id: eos-roadmap -->

<h3>Getting from 60% → 100% EOS</h3>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">Phase 1: Foundation (Days 1–30)</div>
        <ul>
          <li>Audit current EOS implementation — what's real vs. what's on paper</li>
          <li>Attend existing L10 meeting as observer first, then facilitator</li>
          <li>Review the V/TO (Vision/Traction Organizer) with Kevin — is it current?</li>
          <li>Identify who on the team "gets" EOS vs. who's going through the motions</li>
          <li>Assess whether they need an EOS Implementer (outside coach) or if you can drive it internally</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">Phase 2: Accountability Chart & Scorecard (Days 30–60)</div>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Build the Accountability Chart:</strong> Start with the 5–7 major functions. For each seat: name, 5 roles, GWC assessment. This is where you may discover "wrong seat" issues — handle with care.</li>
          <li><strong>Build the Company Scorecard:</strong> 5–15 weekly numbers. For iRESTORE, likely: weekly revenue, units shipped, return rate, CSAT score, inventory weeks-on-hand, Amazon BSR, DTC conversion rate, marketing ROAS, customer service response time.</li>
          <li><strong>Department-level Scorecards:</strong> Each team gets 5–8 numbers they own. Reviewed in their L10.</li>
          <li><strong>Rock-setting:</strong> Review and sharpen existing Rocks. Ensure each is SMART (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound) with a single owner.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">Phase 3: Process & Traction (Days 60–90+)</div>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Core Process Documentation:</strong> Identify the 10–20 core processes that run the business. For iRESTORE: order-to-ship, new product launch, Amazon listing optimization, customer returns, Shenzhen PO process, Q4 peak planning, hiring, etc.</li>
          <li><strong>Document at the 80% level:</strong> EOS doesn't want 100-page SOPs. It wants the 20% of steps that cover 80% of the work. "Followed by all" is the goal.</li>
          <li><strong>Quarterly planning (Annual/Quarterly):</strong> Establish the EOS quarterly planning cadence. Annual planning offsite with Kevin and leadership. Quarterly Rock-setting sessions. This becomes the execution heartbeat.</li>
          <li><strong>Ongoing:</strong> Issues list management, weekly L10 discipline, Rock completion rate tracking (target: 80%+ on-time).</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

      <div class="card">
        <div class="card-header">EOS Books to Reference</div>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Traction</strong> by Gino Wickman — the EOS bible. Kevin has read this.</li>
          <li><strong>Rocket Fuel</strong> by Gino Wickman & Mark C. Winters — specifically about the Visionary/Integrator dynamic. <em>Read this before the interview if you haven't.</em></li>
          <li><strong>Get a Grip</strong> by Gino Wickman — EOS concepts in narrative form (easier to consume).</li>
          <li><strong>How to Be a Great Boss</strong> by Gino Wickman — managing people in EOS.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

## EOS vs. Scaling Up — Which OS for iRESTORE?
<!-- id: eos-vs-scalingup -->

<div class="tip"><strong>Why this matters for your interview:</strong> iRESTORE is at ~$90M and growing 65–80% YoY. EOS was designed for companies in the $1M–$50M range. Scaling Up (Verne Harnish / "Rockefeller Habits 2.0") was built for companies in the $10M–$500M+ range. Kevin chose EOS, but as the business scales past $150M–$200M, you may need to evolve the operating system. Having a point of view on this signals strategic maturity.</div>

<h3>Side-by-Side Comparison</h3>
<table>
  <tr><th>Dimension</th><th>EOS (Traction)</th><th>Scaling Up (Rockefeller Habits)</th></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Creator</strong></td><td>Gino Wickman</td><td>Verne Harnish</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Core Framework</strong></td><td>6 Components: Vision, People, Data, Issues, Process, Traction</td><td>4 Decisions: People, Strategy, Execution, Cash</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Ideal Company Size</strong></td><td>$1M–$50M (10–250 employees). Starts to feel constraining above $50M–$100M for complex orgs.</td><td>$10M–$500M+. Built for companies scaling past founder-led stage into professional management.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Philosophy</strong></td><td><strong>Simplicity.</strong> One way to do everything. Prescriptive and rigid by design — that's the point. "Keep it simple, keep it real."</td><td><strong>Flexibility.</strong> Provides tools and frameworks but lets leadership decide how to implement. More sophisticated, more customizable.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Strategy Depth</strong></td><td>Light. The V/TO (Vision/Traction Organizer) captures vision in 2 pages. No competitive analysis, brand promise, or BHAG equivalent beyond 10-year target.</td><td>Deep. One-Page Strategic Plan (OPSP), SWT analysis, Brand Promise with KPIs, BHAG, 3–5 year strategic moves, profit-per-X metric.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Financial Tools</strong></td><td>Minimal. Scorecard tracks weekly numbers but no cash flow modeling, unit economics, or CCC analysis.</td><td>Strong. Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC), Power of One analysis, Greg Crabtree's "Simple Numbers," Alan Miltz's Cash Flow Story. Cash is an entire pillar.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Meeting Cadence</strong></td><td>Weekly L10 (60 min, strict). Quarterly planning. Annual planning.</td><td>Daily huddle (15 min), Weekly meeting (60–90 min), Monthly management, Quarterly planning, Annual planning. More meetings, more granular.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>People Tools</strong></td><td>Accountability Chart + GWC (Get it, Want it, Capacity). Simple, binary.</td><td>Function Accountability Chart (FACe), Process Accountability Chart (PACe), Topgrading for hiring, A-Player assessments. More nuanced.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Goal Setting</strong></td><td>Rocks (quarterly priorities). 3–7 per company, 3–5 per person.</td><td>Critical Number + quarterly priorities + KPIs at every level. Can layer in OKRs.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Issue Resolution</strong></td><td>IDS (Identify, Discuss, Solve) in L10s. Clean and fast.</td><td>No prescribed method — flexibility to use whatever works. Some use IDS from EOS.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Implementation Time</strong></td><td>3–6 months for core. 12–18 months for full. Lower learning curve.</td><td>6–12 months for core. 18–24 months for full. Steeper learning curve, more sophisticated tools.</td></tr>
  <tr><td><strong>Coach Required?</strong></td><td>EOS Implementer recommended ($50K–$100K/year). Some companies self-implement.</td><td>Scaling Up Coach recommended ($36K–$120K/year). Harder to self-implement due to complexity.</td></tr>
</table>

<h3>Where EOS Falls Short at $90M–$200M+</h3>
<p>EOS is excellent at what it does — creating clarity, rhythm, and accountability. But as iRESTORE scales past $100M, you'll likely hit these limitations:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>No cash management framework:</strong> At $90M with 80% growth, cash is a strategic weapon. EOS doesn't teach cash conversion cycle, working capital optimization, or how to fund growth internally. iRESTORE's net-150 terms from Shenzhen are a massive cash advantage — Scaling Up would help you quantify and optimize this.</li>
  <li><strong>Strategy is too thin:</strong> The V/TO is 2 pages. At $200M+ with international expansion, skincare line launch, B2B channel, and potential acquisition targets, you need more strategic depth than EOS provides. Scaling Up's One-Page Strategic Plan and "7 Strata of Strategy" are built for this.</li>
  <li><strong>No daily huddle:</strong> EOS relies on weekly L10s. At 100+ employees across La Mirada, remote marketing, Philippines, and Shenzhen, a daily 15-minute huddle catches problems 5 days faster. This is one of Scaling Up's most powerful tools.</li>
  <li><strong>Single-layer scorecards:</strong> EOS scorecards work at the company level but get clunky when you need cascading KPIs across multiple departments, channels, and geographies. Scaling Up's approach to cascading metrics is more sophisticated.</li>
  <li><strong>No "profit per X" metric:</strong> Scaling Up asks "what's the one metric that most directly drives profitability?" For iRESTORE, this might be "profit per order" or "LTV per customer acquired." EOS doesn't have this construct.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Where Scaling Up Falls Short for iRESTORE</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Complexity is the enemy of adoption:</strong> iRESTORE is at 60% EOS and the team already knows the language. Switching to Scaling Up means retraining everyone — Rocks become priorities, L10s become weekly meetings, the vocabulary changes. Kevin chose EOS for a reason: it's simple and his team is bought in.</li>
  <li><strong>The Visionary/Integrator framework doesn't exist in Scaling Up:</strong> Kevin thinks of himself as the Visionary and wants you as the Integrator. That's EOS language and EOS structure. Scaling Up doesn't have this distinction — and it's the very framework that defines the COO role he's hiring for.</li>
  <li><strong>Implementation load:</strong> You're joining a $90M company growing 80% YoY with EOS at 60%. The highest-ROI move is completing EOS (getting to 90%+), not starting over with a new system. Scaling Up can come later when the org is stable enough to absorb the complexity.</li>
  <li><strong>Daily huddles may not fit the culture:</strong> iRESTORE's team is lean, distributed, and moving fast. Adding a daily standup to a team that's already stretched could feel bureaucratic. Test it before mandating it.</li>
</ul>

<h3>The Hybrid: EOS Foundation + Scaling Up Upgrades</h3>
<div class="card">
  <div class="card-header">Recommended Approach for iRESTORE</div>
  <p><strong>Phase 1 (Months 1–6): Complete EOS to 90%+.</strong> This is the job Kevin is hiring you for. Finish the Accountability Chart, build scorecards, document core processes, sharpen Rocks, own the L10. Don't introduce new frameworks while EOS is half-baked.</p>
  <p><strong>Phase 2 (Months 6–12): Layer in Scaling Up's financial tools.</strong> Add the Cash Conversion Cycle analysis (critical for a company with net-150 supplier terms and rapid growth). Introduce "Power of One" — what happens if you improve price by 1%, volume by 1%, COGS by 1%, CAC by 1%, DSO by 1 day? This is the CFO-level thinking that EOS lacks.</p>
  <p><strong>Phase 3 (Months 12–18): Upgrade strategy.</strong> When it's time to plan for $200M+, international expansion, and potential M&A, migrate from the V/TO to the One-Page Strategic Plan (OPSP). Keep the EOS execution engine (L10s, Rocks, IDS, Scorecards) but plug in Scaling Up's strategy layer above it. Also consider adding the daily huddle once you have department-level leadership in place.</p>
</div>

<table>
  <tr><th>Keep from EOS</th><th>Add from Scaling Up</th></tr>
  <tr><td>L10 meeting cadence (weekly, Wednesdays)</td><td>Daily huddle (15 min, department level)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Rocks (quarterly priorities)</td><td>Critical Number (one metric per quarter that moves the needle most)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>IDS for issue resolution</td><td>Cash Conversion Cycle + Power of One financial analysis</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Accountability Chart + GWC</td><td>Topgrading for senior hires</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Visionary/Integrator dynamic</td><td>One-Page Strategic Plan (replaces V/TO at $200M+)</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Company & department Scorecards</td><td>Cascading KPIs with "profit per X" metric</td></tr>
  <tr><td>Core Process documentation (80% level)</td><td>Brand Promise with measurable KPIs</td></tr>
</table>

<div class="answer-block"><strong>How to bring this up in the interview:</strong> "I believe in completing EOS first — you've invested in it, the team speaks the language, and getting from 60% to 90% implementation will transform your execution. But I'd be transparent: as we scale past $150M, I think we'll want to layer in some Scaling Up financial tools — particularly around cash conversion and unit economics — because EOS is light on the cash and strategy side. The execution engine stays EOS. The strategic planning and financial rigor evolves. It's not either-or — it's EOS as the operating system with Scaling Up upgrades as we grow into them."</div>

<div class="tip"><strong>Book to reference:</strong> If Kevin asks, mention <em>Scaling Up</em> by Verne Harnish and <em>Mastering the Rockefeller Habits</em> (the earlier version). But position yourself as "EOS-first, evolve when ready" — don't suggest replacing what Kevin built. That's a political mistake.</div>
